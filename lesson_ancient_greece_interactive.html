Democracy, Philosophy & Legends

Welcome to Ancient Greece!

Ancient Greece wasn't one big country like we have today. Instead, it was made up of many independent city-states called "polis." Each city-state had its own government, army, and culture. The two most famous were Athens and Sparta.

Greek civilization flourished from around 800 BC to 146 BC, and during this time, the Greeks gave us incredible gifts that we still use today: democracy, philosophy, theater, the Olympics, and amazing architecture!

The Birth of Democracy üó≥Ô∏è

Around 508 BC, Athens became the world's first democracy. The word "democracy" comes from Greek: demos (people) + kratos (power) = "power to the people"!

How Did Athenian Democracy Work?

The Assembly (Ekklesia)

All male citizens could attend and vote on laws. They met 40 times per year on a hill called the Pnyx. About 6,000 citizens usually attended.

The Council of 500

500 citizens chosen by lottery each year. They prepared laws for the Assembly to vote on. Any citizen over 30 could serve.

The Law Courts

Juries of hundreds of citizens decided legal cases. No judges or lawyers - citizens spoke for themselves!

Ostracism

Once a year, citizens could vote to exile someone for 10 years if they thought that person was dangerous to democracy. They wrote names on pottery pieces called "ostraka."

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Limitations

Athenian democracy was revolutionary, but it wasn't fair to everyone:

‚ùå Women could not vote or participate in politics

‚ùå Slaves (about 1/3 of the population) had no rights

‚ùå Foreigners (called "metics") could not be citizens even if they lived in Athens their whole lives

‚úì Only free adult male citizens (born in Athens with Athenian parents) could vote

The Great Philosophers üß†

Ancient Greece gave us some of history's greatest thinkers. They asked big questions about life, truth, justice, and how we should live. Their ideas still influence us today!

The Three Greatest Philosophers

1. Socrates (469-399 BC)

Famous For: Asking questions to make people think deeply

Main Ideas: "The unexamined life is not worth living" - we must question everything. He believed that knowledge leads to virtue, and that admitting ignorance is the first step to wisdom.

Teaching Method: The "Socratic Method" - teaching by asking questions instead of giving answers.

Death: Athens sentenced him to death for "corrupting the youth" and questioning traditional beliefs. He drank poison (hemlock) rather than escape.

2. Plato (428-348 BC)

Student Of: Socrates

Famous For: Writing dialogues (conversations) featuring Socrates

Main Ideas: The "Theory of Forms" - everything we see is just an imperfect copy of a perfect ideal. For example, all chairs are imperfect copies of the perfect "Form of Chair."

Founded: The Academy in Athens - the world's first university!

Political Ideas: Wrote "The Republic" about the ideal society ruled by "philosopher-kings."

3. Aristotle (384-322 BC)

Student Of: Plato

Famous For: Studying everything! Biology, physics, politics, ethics, logic, poetry...

Main Ideas: Knowledge comes from observing the real world (unlike Plato who focused on perfect Forms). Created formal logic. Studied the "Golden Mean" - virtue is found between extremes (courage is between cowardice and recklessness).

Student: Tutored Alexander the Great!

Founded: The Lyceum - another famous school in Athens.

Greek Mythology ‚ö°

The ancient Greeks believed in many gods and goddesses who lived on Mount Olympus and controlled different aspects of life and nature. These myths explained everything from why the seasons change to why lightning strikes!

The 12 Olympian Gods

‚ö° Zeus - King of the Gods

God of: Sky, thunder, lightning, law and order

Symbol: Lightning bolt, eagle

Personality: Powerful but unfaithful to his wife Hera. Could be merciful or wrathful.

üåä Poseidon - God of the Sea

God of: Oceans, earthquakes, horses

Symbol: Trident (three-pronged spear)

Personality: Moody like the ocean - calm or stormy. Zeus's brother.

üåæ Athena - Goddess of Wisdom

Goddess of: Wisdom, war strategy, crafts

Symbol: Owl, olive tree

Special: Born from Zeus's head fully grown and armored! Patron goddess of Athens.

üèπ Apollo - God of the Sun

God of: Sun, music, poetry, prophecy, medicine

Symbol: Lyre (musical instrument), sun chariot

Personality: Beautiful and talented. Zeus's son.

ü¶å Artemis - Goddess of the Hunt

Goddess of: Hunting, wild animals, the moon

Symbol: Bow and arrow, deer

Special: Apollo's twin sister. Protector of young girls. Chose to never marry.

‚öîÔ∏è Ares - God of War

God of: War, violence, bloodshed

Symbol: Spear, helmet, dog

Personality: Aggressive and brutal. Unlike Athena (strategic warfare), Ares loved the chaos of battle.

üìñ Famous Greek Myths - Quick Summary

The 12 Labors of Hercules

Hercules (a half-god) had to complete 12 impossible tasks as punishment, including killing the Nemean Lion, slaying the nine-headed Hydra, and capturing Cerberus (the three-headed dog guarding the Underworld).

The Trojan War

A 10-year war between Greece and Troy, started because Paris (a Trojan prince) stole Helen (the most beautiful woman in the world) from her Greek husband. The Greeks won by hiding soldiers inside a giant wooden horse.

Pandora's Box

The first woman, Pandora, was given a box and told never to open it. Her curiosity got the better of her, and when she opened it, all the evils of the world flew out (disease, war, sadness, etc.). Only Hope remained inside.

Odysseus's Journey Home

After the Trojan War, Odysseus took 10 years to sail home, facing monsters like the Cyclops, the Sirens (whose songs drove sailors mad), and Scylla and Charybdis (sea monsters).

Daily Life in Ancient Greece üè∫

What was it really like to live in Ancient Greece? Let's explore how ordinary people spent their days!

A Day in the Life...

üåÖ Dawn (6:00 AM)

Wake Up! Greeks woke with the sunrise. Breakfast was simple: bread dipped in wine, olives, figs.

‚òÄÔ∏è Morning (7:00 AM - 12:00 PM)

Men: Went to work (farming, crafts, trade) or to the agora (marketplace) to discuss politics and philosophy.

Women: Managed the household, supervised slaves, wove cloth, prepared food.

Children: Boys went to school (reading, writing, music, sports). Girls learned home skills from their mothers.

üåû Midday (12:00 PM - 2:00 PM)

Lunch & Rest: The main meal of the day. Then a siesta during the hottest hours.

üå§Ô∏è Afternoon (2:00 PM - 6:00 PM)

Men: The gymnasium (exercise, sports, socializing) or public baths.

Women: Continued household work, rarely left the house.

Children: More school or play (ball games, knucklebones, dolls).

üåÜ Evening (6:00 PM - 10:00 PM)

Dinner & Entertainment: Men often attended symposiums (drinking parties with philosophical discussions). Women ate separately. Theater performances were popular events!

üåô Night (10:00 PM)

Bedtime: No street lights! People went to bed early. Houses were lit with oil lamps.

üé≠ Entertainment & Leisure

Theater

Greeks invented theater! They watched tragedies (serious plays about heroes) and comedies (funny plays making fun of politicians). Famous playwrights: Sophocles, Euripides, Aristophanes.

Olympic Games

Every 4 years, athletes competed in running, wrestling, boxing, chariot racing, and more. Winners got olive wreaths (not gold medals!). During the games, all Greek city-states stopped their wars!

Symposiums

Drinking parties for men only, where they discussed philosophy, recited poetry, and played music. Women were not invited (except entertainers).
