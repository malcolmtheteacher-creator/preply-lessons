Meaning:

2. "The singularity"
Meaning:

A hypothetical future point when AI becomes smarter than humans and can improve itself, leading to rapid, unpredictable changes

Context:

Used in: AI ethics, futurism, technology philosophy, science fiction discussions

Why "singularity"?:

Like a black hole in physicsâ€”a point beyond which we can't predict what happens. Once AI surpasses human intelligence, the future becomes unknowable.

Controversy:

Some think it's imminent (2030s-2040s). Others think it's impossible or centuries away. Nobody knows.

Example 1 (Future prediction):

"If we reach the singularityâ€”a point where AI can recursively improve itselfâ€”human intelligence becomes as relevant to AI as insect intelligence is to us."

Critical thinking:

Do you think the singularity will happen? Should we plan as if it will, or ignore it as science fiction?

3. "To augment human capability"

6. "Human agency"
Meaning:

These 6 expressions help you discuss the unknown future, make predictions, evaluate possibilities, and think about long-term consequencesâ€”essential for AI discussions.

6 C2 Expressions

7. "It's conceivable that..."
Meaning:

It's possible to imagine or believe; it could potentially happen (though not certain)

Context:

Used in: speculation, possibility discussions, future predictions

Formality:

Academic/formal. Shows sophisticated thinking about possibilities without claiming certainty.

Similar to:

"It's plausible that..." / "One could imagine..." / "It's within the realm of possibility..."

Example 1 (AI future):

"It's conceivable that within 20 years, AI could perform most intellectual work better than humans, fundamentally reshaping the entire economy and job market."

Example 2 (Hedged prediction):

"While I don't think mass unemployment is inevitable, it's conceivable that automation could displace workers faster than new industries can absorb them, creating a transition crisis."

Why use it:

Shows you're thinking about possibilities without making absolute claims. It's intellectually honestâ€”acknowledging uncertainty while considering scenarios.

8. "Extrapolating from current trends..."
Meaning:

Making predictions about the future by extending current patterns forward; assuming what's happening now will continue

Context:

Used in: forecasting, analysis, strategic planning, research

The method:

Look at how things are changing now â†’ Project that change into the future â†’ Predict outcome

Limitation:

Assumes trends continue, but reality often has disruptions, inflection points, or paradigm shifts.

Example 1 (AI capability growth):

"Extrapolating from current trends in AI developmentâ€”where capabilities double every 18 monthsâ€”we could see human-level AI in most domains within a decade."

Example 2 (Critical analysis):

"Extrapolating from current trends suggests massive job displacement, but this assumes no policy intervention, no new industries emerging, and no social adaptationâ€”which seems unlikely."

In arguments:

Use this to show you're basing predictions on evidence (current data), not random speculation.

9. "In all likelihood..."
Meaning:

Probably; most likely; with high probability

Context:

Used in: predictions, probability discussions, reasoned forecasts

Confidence level:

Stronger than "possibly" or "maybe," weaker than "certainly" or "definitely." Suggests ~70-80% confidence.

Function:

Shows you're making educated guesses based on reasoning, not wild speculation or absolute certainty.

Example 1 (Realistic prediction):

"In all likelihood, most current jobs won't disappear entirelyâ€”they'll transform. Humans will work alongside AI, focusing on tasks machines can't do well."

Example 2 (Hedged forecast):

"In all likelihood, some form of Universal Basic Income will become necessary if automation continues at current pace. The political will may not be there yet, but economic necessity will force the conversation."

Nuance:

This phrase shows intellectual humilityâ€”you're confident but not arrogant. You acknowledge uncertainty while offering your best judgment.

10. "A plausible scenario"
Meaning:

A possible future outcome that seems reasonable and believable, even if not certain

Context:

Used in: scenario planning, strategic thinking, possibility analysis

What makes a scenario plausible?:

Based on current evidence, follows logical cause-and-effect, doesn't require miraculous interventions or impossibilities

Multiple scenarios:

Often used to present several plausible futures, acknowledging we can't predict with certainty which will occur.

Example 1 (Future possibility):

"A plausible scenario is that AI creates massive wealth but it's concentrated among tech company owners, leading to extreme inequality and social unrest within 10-15 years."

Example 2 (Balanced analysis):

"Both utopian and dystopian futures are plausible scenarios. The question isn't which is 'right' but rather which policy choices make positive outcomes more likely."

In discussions:

Use this to present possibilities without claiming certainty. Shows sophisticated thinkingâ€”acknowledging multiple potential futures.

11. "To hedge one's bets"
Meaning:

To protect yourself against risk by pursuing multiple options simultaneously; to not put all your eggs in one basket

Context:

Used in: risk management, decision-making, strategy, investment

Origin:

From betting/gamblingâ€”placing multiple bets to reduce the chance of total loss

AI career application:

Don't commit to one career pathâ€”develop diverse skills because you don't know which jobs will survive automation.

Example 1 (Career strategy):

"I'm hedging my bets by learning both programming and creative writing. If one gets automated, I have a fallback. I'm not confident either is safe long-term."

Example 2 (Education choices):

"Many students are hedging their bets with double majorsâ€”pairing technical skills with human-centered ones like psychology or philosophy, hoping the combination makes them automation-proof."

Practical wisdom:

In uncertain times, hedging bets is rational. The cost of being wrong about your single bet is too high.

12. "The trajectory suggests..."
Meaning:

The current path or direction of development indicates/points toward a particular outcome

Context:

Used in: trend analysis, forecasting, strategic planning

What's a trajectory?:

The path something followsâ€”like a thrown ball. Implies momentum and direction that will continue unless something changes it.

Critical note:

Trajectories can change! Just because something is heading in a direction doesn't mean it will stay that way.

Example 1 (AI development):

"The trajectory suggests that AI capabilities will continue exponential growth. If this holds, human-level AI across most domains could arrive sooner than most people expect."

Example 2 (Counter-argument):

"While the trajectory suggests widespread automation, we've been here before. Every technological revolution creates new jobs we couldn't imagine. The trajectory isn't destiny."

In analysis:

Shows you're basing predictions on observed patterns, not guesses. But leaves room for changeâ€”trajectories can shift.
