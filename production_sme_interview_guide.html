Production & Publishing Interview Guide

Career Advancement in Academic & STM Publishing

Understanding Production Leadership Interviews

What Publishers Look For in Senior Production Candidates:

Research shows that academic publishers emphasize behavioral questions significantly more than other industries. They want to see:
  • Real examples of how you've handled situations
  • Decision-making ability with strategic thinking
  • Stakeholder management across functions
  • Process improvement mindset
  • Technical depth in your area of specialization

Coordinator vs. Senior Leadership: Key Differences

Production Coordinator Senior Production Role / Expert
Manages assigned portfolio Shapes systems and processes for entire team
Follows established workflows Evaluates and improves workflows
Escalates system issues Translates production needs into requirements
Solves problems as they arise Prevents problems through better systems
Individual contributor focus Team leadership and knowledge sharing
Executes tasks Makes strategic decisions on behalf of production

Typical Interview Structure

Stage What to Expect Duration
1. HR Screening Background, motivation, salary expectations 20-30 min
2. Assignment Prioritization, email writing, evaluation task (common in publishing) 30-60 min
3. Team Lead Interview Behavioral questions, technical scenarios, SME thinking 45-60 min
4. Panel/Director Interview Strategic fit, leadership potential, culture alignment 30-45 min
Making the Transition from Execution to Strategy:

Moving from coordinator to senior/leadership roles requires demonstrating a different way of thinking. Interviewers will assess:
  • Can you articulate why THIS specific role appeals to you (beyond title or salary)?
  • Do you understand how leadership responsibilities differ from execution?
  • Can you discuss process improvements without criticizing how things currently work?
  • Are you ready for the complexity of balancing hands-on work with strategic contributions?
Strong candidates show they've already been thinking at the next level—identifying patterns, proposing improvements, and considering team-wide impact.

Key Responsibilities in Senior Production Roles

70% Expert Work

  • System development and advocacy
  • Decision-making authority for production
  • Training and documentation
  • Cross-functional representation
  • Process standardization
  • Knowledge sharing

30% Coordination Work

  • Managing journal portfolio
  • Staying current with workflows
  • Modeling best practices
  • Covering during peaks/absences
  • Testing improvements
  • Maintaining credibility
Common Interview Mistakes to Avoid:
  • ❌ Giving vague answers without specific examples
  • ❌ Focusing only on "what" you did, not "why" or "how you decided"
  • ❌ Talking about team achievements using "we" instead of your individual contributions
  • ❌ Not quantifying results ("improved quality" vs. "reduced errors by 25%")
  • ❌ Showing coordinator thinking when they want strategic/leadership thinking
  • ❌ Being unprepared for scenario-based questions

The STAR Method for Publishing Interviews

Why STAR?

STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) is the industry-standard framework for behavioral interviews. Publishers use it because it provides structure while allowing you to tell complete stories that demonstrate your capabilities.

The STAR Framework

Component Purpose Time What to Include
Situation Set context 20 sec When, where, what circumstances? Keep brief.
Task Define challenge 15 sec What needed to be accomplished? Why was it challenging?
Action Show your process 60-90 sec What YOU did (use "I" not "we"), your decision-making, why you chose this approach
Result Prove impact 30 sec Measurable outcomes, what you learned, what you'd do differently

Making Your "Action" Section Strategic

The Action section is where SME thinking shines. Don't just list what you did—show HOW you thought through the problem:

✓ "I considered three approaches..." (shows you evaluate options)
✓ "I decided to X because..." (shows reasoning)
✓ "I consulted with [stakeholders]..." (shows collaboration)
✓ "I prioritized Y over Z because..." (shows judgment)
✓ "Here's specifically what I did..." (shows execution)

This natural enrichment demonstrates strategic thinking without a forced structure.

Example: Weak vs. Strong STAR

❌ Weak Example

S: We had a quality issue with a journal.

T: We needed to fix it.

A: We worked with the vendor to resolve it.

R: It got fixed and quality improved.


Problems: Vague, uses "we", no specifics, no decision-making shown, no numbers

✓ Strong Example

S: Last quarter, one of my journals had a 15% error rate in references—triple our standard.

T: I needed to identify root cause and prevent recurrence while maintaining publication schedule.

A: I analyzed 20 recent articles and found the vendor was misinterpreting our style guide. I considered three options: changing vendors (disruptive), accepting lower quality (unacceptable), or fixing the process. I chose to revise our vendor brief with specific examples and conducted a 30-minute training session with their team. I also implemented spot-checks on 10% of articles.

R: Error rate dropped to 3% within one month and stayed there. The improved brief became our team standard. I learned to invest time upfront in vendor clarity rather than fixing errors later.

Practice: Convert This to STAR

Scenario: You improved a workflow that saved time. Structure this using STAR:

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Pitfall How to Avoid
Using "we" instead of "I" Practice saying "I led the team..." "I decided..." "I coordinated..." Even in team situations, focus on YOUR role
Rambling Action section Have 3-4 key points max. Structure: "I did A, then B, then C" not a stream of consciousness
Vague results Always quantify: time saved, error reduction %, cost impact, efficiency gain
Not showing learning End every Result with: "What I learned..." or "If I faced this again, I'd..."
Memorizing word-for-word Know your key points but tell the story naturally. Memorized answers sound robotic

Build Your Interview Stories

Story Bank Strategy:

Prepare 4-5 strong stories that cover different competencies. Choose stories that:
  • Happened in the last 2-3 years (recent and relevant)
  • Show clear results with numbers
  • Demonstrate SME-level thinking
  • Can be adapted to answer multiple questions

The 4 Essential Story Categories

Category 1: Stakeholder Management

What they're assessing: Can you balance competing demands, manage difficult relationships, and communicate effectively?

Possible scenarios:

  • Handling a demanding author or editor
  • Negotiating with vendors on quality or deadlines
  • Navigating conflicting priorities from different teams
  • Building relationships with editorial/technology partners

Category 2: Problem-Solving Under Pressure

What they're assessing: How do you handle crises, make decisions with incomplete information, and maintain quality under deadlines?

Possible scenarios:

  • Production crisis threatening publication deadline
  • Systematic quality issue discovered late in process
  • Vendor failure requiring quick alternative solution
  • Technical problem affecting multiple journals

Category 3: Process Improvement

What they're assessing: Do you proactively identify inefficiencies and drive standardization? Can you implement lasting change?

Possible scenarios:

  • Streamlining a repetitive workflow
  • Standardizing practices across journals
  • Proposing system enhancements
  • Reducing costs while maintaining quality

Category 4: Leadership & Influence

What they're assessing: Can you lead without authority, train others, and advocate for production needs in strategic discussions?

Possible scenarios:

  • Training or mentoring team members
  • Influencing stakeholders who don't report to you
  • Leading a cross-functional project
  • Advocating for production requirements with editorial/tech
After Writing Your Stories:
  1. Practice out loud with a friend or teacher - telling is different from writing
  2. Time yourself - aim for 2-3 minutes per story
  3. Prepare for follow-ups - interviewers will dig deeper on any detail
  4. Be ready to adapt - the same story might answer different questions
  5. Have numbers ready - know your metrics cold

Common Interview Questions

Question Strategy: Publishers commonly ask scenario-based behavioral questions. They want to hear real examples, not theoretical answers. Every response should include a concrete story from your experience.

Standard Behavioral Questions

"Tell me about yourself"

Structure (2-3 minutes):

  1. Current role (40 sec): What you do, key responsibilities, portfolio scale
  2. Key achievement (40 sec): One specific accomplishment with numbers
  3. Why this role (40 sec): What excites you about the SME opportunity
  4. Unique value (20 sec): What you bring that others might not
Example:

"I'm currently a Production Coordinator at [Publisher], where I manage a portfolio of 15 journals through their complete production lifecycle. I coordinate with vendors, ensure quality standards, and work closely with editorial teams to meet publication deadlines.

One achievement I'm proud of is implementing a streamlined vendor briefing process that reduced our average turnaround time by 18% while actually improving quality scores. I noticed we were losing time in the handoff between editorial and production, so I created standardized templates and trained both teams on the new workflow.

What excites me about this senior role is the opportunity to work at a strategic level—not just managing my journals, but helping shape the systems and processes that benefit the entire production team. I've been working with [relevant systems] for four years, and I'm energized by the idea of being the go-to expert and contributing to system development.

What I bring is a combination of hands-on production expertise and a passion for process improvement. I don't just execute workflows—I'm constantly evaluating how we can work smarter and more efficiently."

Stakeholder Management Questions

Question What They're Really Asking
"Tell me about a time you had to work with a difficult stakeholder" Can you handle conflict professionally? Do you understand different perspectives?
"Describe a situation where you had competing priorities from different teams" How do you make trade-off decisions? Can you say no diplomatically?
"Give me an example of when you had to influence someone without authority" Can you lead through persuasion? Do you build coalitions?
"Tell me about a time you had to deliver bad news to a stakeholder" Are you transparent? Can you manage expectations? Do you provide solutions?

Problem-Solving Questions

Question What They're Really Asking
"Tell me about a time you faced a tight deadline that seemed impossible" How do you handle pressure? Can you prioritize ruthlessly? Do you stay calm?
"Describe a situation where you had to solve a problem with limited information" Are you decisive? Do you make reasonable assumptions? How do you manage risk?
"Give me an example of a quality issue you discovered and resolved" Are you detail-oriented? Do you identify root causes? Can you prevent recurrence?
"Tell me about a time when your solution to a problem didn't work" Do you learn from failure? Are you adaptable? Can you course-correct?

Process Improvement Questions

Question What They're Really Asking
"Tell me about a time you identified an opportunity to improve a process" Do you think critically about workflows? Are you proactive? Can you implement change?
"Describe how you've contributed to standardization efforts" Do you think beyond your desk? Can you scale solutions? Do you think strategically?
"Give me an example of when you proposed a change that was initially resisted" Are you persistent? Can you build consensus? Do you handle pushback well?

Senior Role Questions

"Why do you want to move into a senior/leadership role?"

Strong answers include:
  • ✓ Desire to have strategic impact beyond individual journals
  • ✓ Interest in system development and process improvement
  • ✓ Passion for training and knowledge sharing
  • ✓ Readiness for decision-making authority and responsibility
  • ✓ Specific examples of times you've already demonstrated leadership/expert thinking

Avoid:
  • ✗ "I want a promotion" (focus on the role, not the title)
  • ✗ "I've been here X years" (tenure doesn't equal readiness)
  • ✗ "I'm good at my current job" (leadership roles require different skills)
  • ✗ Vague answers without specifics

"How would you balance the 70% expert work with 30% coordination work?"

Key points to address:
  • ✓ The 30% keeps you credible and current
  • ✓ You'd use your coordination work strategically (testing improvements, modeling best practices)
  • ✓ How you'd prioritize when there are conflicts (hard deadlines, scope of impact)
  • ✓ The 70% is where you create strategic value for the organization
  • ✓ You'd protect the expert time and communicate proactively when needed

Questions YOU Should Ask

Your questions reveal your thinking level. Ask strategic questions:

Good questions:
  • "What are the biggest production challenges the team is facing, and how could this senior role help?"
  • "How do senior production staff currently contribute to system development roadmaps?"
  • "What does success look like for this role in the first 6 months?"
  • "How does the production team collaborate across different specializations?"
  • "What opportunities exist for growth into team lead or other roles?"

Avoid questions that sound self-focused too early:
  • ❌ "What training will I receive?"
  • ❌ "What's the work-from-home policy?"
  • ❌ "How much travel is required?"
(These are valid but save them for after you've demonstrated your value)

Tackling Interview Assignments

Why Publishers Use Assignments:

Timed assignments (30-60 minutes) are common in publishing production interviews. They assess how you:
  • Prioritize competing demands
  • Communicate professionally
  • Make decisions with realistic constraints
  • Think about quality vs. efficiency trade-offs

Common Assignment Types

Type 1: Prioritization Exercise

Scenario: You're given 10 tasks with competing deadlines and must rank them.

What they're assessing: Decision-making framework, ability to justify choices, understanding of production priorities

Approach:
  1. Identify hard deadlines (publication dates) vs. soft deadlines (internal milestones)
  2. Assess impact: What affects authors/editors vs. internal workflow?
  3. Consider dependencies: What's blocking other work?
  4. Factor in effort: Quick wins vs. time-intensive tasks
  5. Document your reasoning for each decision
Framework: Urgent + Important Matrix
• Critical & Urgent (do first): Publication deadlines, quality issues affecting readers
• Important not Urgent (schedule): Process improvements, training
• Urgent not Important (delegate/defer): Routine admin if others can handle
• Neither (drop): Low-value tasks

Type 2: Email Communication

Scenario: Write an email to a stakeholder (author, editor, vendor) about a problem.

What they're assessing: Professional tone, clear communication, problem-solving approach, diplomacy

Email Structure:
  1. Subject line: Clear, specific (not vague "Update")
  2. Opening: Professional greeting + immediate context
  3. Problem: State the issue clearly and objectively
  4. Solution: What you're proposing or need from them
  5. Timeline: When this needs to happen
  6. Close: Offer to discuss, thank them
Tone Guidelines:
• Be direct but diplomatic (no blame)
• Use "we" for shared goals, "I" for your actions
• Focus on solutions, not just problems
• Be specific about what you need
• Proofread for typos (they're assessing attention to detail)

Type 3: Evaluation Task

Scenario: Review a submission/manuscript and provide assessment or editorial recommendations.

What they're assessing: Analytical thinking, attention to detail, ability to articulate reasoning, judgment

Approach:
  1. Skim first: Get overall sense before detailed review
  2. Use criteria: Quality, scope fit, technical soundness, clarity
  3. Document issues: Note specific problems with examples
  4. Make recommendation: Accept/revise/reject with clear rationale
  5. Be constructive: If revisions needed, be specific about what
Structure Your Response:
• Summary: Overall assessment in 2-3 sentences
• Strengths: What works well (be specific)
• Concerns: Issues identified (with examples)
• Recommendation: Your decision with reasoning
• Next steps: What should happen

Assignment Best Practices

Do Don't
✓ Read all instructions carefully before starting ✗ Rush in without understanding the full scope
✓ Manage your time (check clock regularly) ✗ Spend 40 minutes on the first of three tasks
✓ Show your reasoning explicitly ✗ Just provide answers without explanation
✓ Use professional formatting ✗ Submit messy or hard-to-read work
✓ Proofread before submitting ✗ Submit first draft with typos
✓ Make realistic decisions (they expect trade-offs) ✗ Try to make everyone happy or do everything perfectly
Time Management Tips:
  • For 60-minute assignment with 3 tasks: spend 18 min each + 6 min review
  • Set mental timers: "I'll finish task 1 by minute 18"
  • If you're stuck, move on and come back if time permits
  • Better to complete all tasks adequately than one perfectly and two poorly
  • Reserve last 5 minutes for proofreading

During Interview: Real-Time Tactics

Situation What to Do
You don't understand a question "Just to make sure I understand - are you asking about [rephrase]?"
You need time to think "That's a great question. Let me take a moment to structure my thoughts." [Pause 10-15 seconds]
You realize you're rambling STOP. Take breath. "Let me refocus on the key point..." Then summarize concisely.
You don't know something "I haven't encountered that specifically, but here's how I'd approach learning it..." or "...here's my experience with something similar..."
They challenge your answer "That's a fair point. Let me think about that from another angle..." Show openness to other perspectives.
You make a factual mistake "Actually, let me correct that - [accurate info]." Own it quickly, move on. Don't dwell.

The Follow-Up Email (24-Hour Rule)

Send within 24 hours (ideally 12 hours):

Subject: Thank you - [Your Name] - [Position] Interview

Structure (150-200 words total):

Paragraph 1: Thank them + reference specific discussion point
"Thank you for taking time to discuss the Senior Production role yesterday. I particularly appreciated our conversation about [specific topic they mentioned]."

Paragraph 2: Reiterate interest + add value
"Our discussion reinforced my enthusiasm for the position. After reflecting on [something they mentioned], I wanted to share [relevant insight/article]. My experience with [relevant skill] would allow me to contribute immediately to [team goal they mentioned]."

Paragraph 3: Express enthusiasm + next steps
"I'm excited about the possibility of joining the production team. Please let me know if you need any additional information. I look forward to hearing about next steps."
