Why We Stay (When We Should Leave)

Real Conversations Series | C1-C2 Level | 50 minutes

You knew 6 months ago.

You knew it was time to leave. The job wasn't right. The relationship was over. The friendship had turned toxic. You could feel it in your bones.

But here you are. A year later. Still there. Still stuck.

Why do we stay in situations we know are wrong for us?

Real Story #1: Emma's Sunk Cost

Emma, 34, Marketing Manager
Stayed 5 years in a toxic workplace

Emma joined a marketing agency excited about her career. Within 6 months, she knew it was wrong. Her boss was a micromanager who criticized everything. The hours were brutal‚Äî60+ weeks were normal. She cried in the bathroom at least once a week.

But she stayed. And stayed. And stayed.

"I kept thinking: 'I've already invested two years here. If I leave now, it was all for nothing.' Then it was three years. Then four. By year five, I was so burned out I could barely function. The day I finally quit, I felt like I'd escaped prison."

Why she stayed: Sunk cost fallacy. She kept investing time hoping it would get better, not wanting to "waste" the time already spent.

The cost: Five years of misery, therapy for anxiety, damaged confidence, missed opportunities at other companies.

Real Story #2: James's Comfortable Cage

James, 41, IT Consultant
Stayed 8 years in a dying relationship

James and his girlfriend had been together since college. Somewhere around year 5, the spark died. They stopped having real conversations. They became roommates who occasionally had sex. He knew it wasn't working.

Friends asked him: "Are you happy?" He'd say: "It's complicated."

"The truth? I was terrified. We had a nice apartment. Our lives were intertwined. Starting over at 40 seemed impossible. What if I never found anyone else? What if I was making a huge mistake? So I just... stayed. We both did. Until she finally ended it."

Why he stayed: Fear of the unknown. Comfort zone. Easier to stay in mediocrity than risk the unknown.

The cost: Eight years of both their lives. His ex is now happily married to someone else. He wishes he'd been brave enough to leave sooner.

Real Story #3: Sarah's "What Will They Think?"

Sarah, 29, Teacher
Stayed 3 years in the wrong career

Sarah's entire family were teachers. Her mother was a teacher. Her grandmother was a teacher. Everyone was so proud when she got her teaching degree. There was just one problem: she hated teaching.

She loved kids, but the bureaucracy, the paperwork, the lack of autonomy‚Äîit crushed her. She dreamed of starting a business, but...

"How could I tell my family I wanted to quit? They'd think I was throwing away my education. My mom would be devastated. My friends would think I was irresponsible. So I kept teaching, and every year I felt more trapped. I finally left at 29, but I wish I'd done it at 25."

Why she stayed: Fear of disappointing others. Social pressure. "What will people think?"

The cost: Three years of depression, feeling like a fraud, anxiety every Sunday night.

The Four Reasons We Stay

üß† The Psychology

1. Sunk Cost Fallacy - "I've already invested so much time/money/energy. I can't quit now."

2. Fear of the Unknown - "What if the next job/relationship/place is worse? At least I know what I have."

3. Comfort Zone - "It's not great, but it's familiar. Change is hard."

4. Social Pressure - "What will people think? My family will be disappointed. I'll look like a failure."

Expressions 1-6: Talking About Being Stuck

1. "I'm torn between..."
Neutral

Meaning:

You're stuck between two options and can't decide. Both have advantages and disadvantages. You feel pulled in two directions.

When to use it:

When discussing difficult decisions where both options are valid but you can't choose.

In context (Emma's story):
"I was torn between staying at the company where I'd invested 5 years and starting over somewhere new. Part of me thought 'You've come this far, just stick it out.' But another part was screaming 'Get out before it destroys you.'"
Other examples:
‚Ä¢ "I'm torn between taking the promotion and keeping my work-life balance."
‚Ä¢ "She's torn between staying in her hometown and moving abroad for better opportunities."
‚Ä¢ "I was torn between being honest with him and not wanting to hurt his feelings."
2. "What's holding me back is..."
Neutral

Click to see meaning and example ‚Üí

Meaning:

You identify the specific fear, obstacle, or reason that prevents you from taking action. It's an honest admission of what stops you.

When to use it:

When you want to be honest about your fears or the real reasons you haven't made a change.

In context (James's story):
"What's holding me back is the fear of being alone at 40. I know the relationship isn't working, but at least I have someone. Starting over terrifies me more than staying in something mediocre."
Other examples:
‚Ä¢ "What's holding me back is the financial risk. I have a mortgage and kids."
‚Ä¢ "Honestly, what's holding me back is caring too much about what my parents will think."
‚Ä¢ "What's holding me back is the comfort. I know exactly what to expect here."
3. "I keep telling myself..."
Informal

Click to see meaning and example ‚Üí

Meaning:

You're repeating a justification or excuse to yourself, often to avoid taking action. It reveals self-deception or rationalization.

When to use it:

When admitting you're making excuses or trying to convince yourself of something you don't fully believe.

In context (Emma's story):
"I kept telling myself 'It'll get better after this project' or 'Once I get promoted, things will change' or 'Maybe I'm just not tough enough.' But deep down, I knew none of that was true. I was just scared to leave."
Other examples:
‚Ä¢ "I keep telling myself I'll quit next year, but next year never comes."
‚Ä¢ "She keeps telling herself he'll change, but he's shown her who he is."
‚Ä¢ "I kept telling myself 'I'm learning so much' but really, I was just stalling."
4. "I've invested so much in..."
Neutral

Click to see meaning and example ‚Üí

Meaning:

You've put significant time, money, energy, or emotion into something. This often keeps people stuck because they don't want the investment to be "wasted."

When to use it:

When explaining why it's hard to walk away, or discussing the sunk cost fallacy.

In context (Emma's story):
"I'd invested so much in that company‚Äî5 years of my life, countless hours of overtime, my entire network was there. Walking away felt like admitting defeat, like all those years were for nothing."
Other examples:
‚Ä¢ "I've invested so much in this degree, I feel like I have to use it even though I hate the field."
‚Ä¢ "We've invested so much in this house. Selling it now would be admitting we made a mistake."
‚Ä¢ "He's invested so much emotional energy in that friendship. It's hard for him to accept it's toxic."
5. "At least I know what I'm dealing with"
Informal

Click to see meaning and example ‚Üí

Meaning:

The situation may not be good, but it's familiar and predictable. The unknown is scarier than the known problems.

When to use it:

When explaining why you're choosing familiarity over uncertainty, even if the current situation isn't great.

In context (James's story):
"I thought: 'Sure, the relationship isn't exciting anymore, but at least I know what I'm dealing with. We don't fight much. It's comfortable. Starting over with someone new? That's a complete gamble. What if it's worse?'"
Other examples:
‚Ä¢ "My job isn't great, but at least I know what I'm dealing with. The workload is predictable."
‚Ä¢ "This apartment is too small, but at least we know what we're dealing with. A new place could have hidden problems."
‚Ä¢ "The boss is difficult, but at least I know how to manage him. A new boss could be even worse."
6. "The thought of... terrifies me"
Neutral

Click to see meaning and example ‚Üí

Meaning:

You're admitting a deep fear. "Terrifies" is strong‚Äîit's not just worry, it's genuine fear that keeps you paralyzed.

When to use it:

When being honest about what really scares you and holds you back from action.

In context (Sarah's story):
"The thought of disappointing my mother terrified me. She'd sacrificed so much for my education. How could I tell her I wanted to throw it all away and start a business? The conversation we'd have to have kept me up at night."
Other examples:
‚Ä¢ "The thought of being a single parent terrifies me, so I stay even though I'm unhappy."
‚Ä¢ "Starting over in a new city where I don't know anyone‚Äîthe thought terrifies me."
‚Ä¢ "The thought of failing publicly terrifies me more than never trying at all."

Expressions 7-12: Talking About Leaving & Liberation
