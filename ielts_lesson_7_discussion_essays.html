What is a Discussion Essay?

A discussion essay asks you to explore both sides of an argument before (usually) giving your own opinion. Unlike opinion essays where you argue for ONE side, discussion essays require you to:

‚úì Present View A fairly

Explain the first perspective with reasons and examples, using neutral language.

‚úì Present View B fairly

Explain the opposite perspective equally well, also with neutral language.

‚úì Give your opinion (if asked)

State which view you support, or find middle ground - but only if the question asks for your opinion!

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Difference from Opinion Essays:
Opinion Essay Discussion Essay
Take ONE side strongly Discuss BOTH sides fairly
Both body paragraphs support your view Each body paragraph covers a different view
Opinion throughout entire essay Neutral until opinion section (if asked)
Use persuasive language Use neutral, balanced language

Why Discussion Essays Are Challenging

Common Mistakes Students Make:
  • ‚ùå Writing more about one view than the other (unbalanced)
  • ‚ùå Giving opinion too early (should stay neutral in body paragraphs)
  • ‚ùå Only discussing one side properly
  • ‚ùå Confusing it with an opinion essay
  • ‚ùå Not answering both parts of the question
  • ‚ùå Using biased language when presenting views
‚úÖ What Examiners Want to See:
  • ‚úì Both views discussed equally (similar length)
  • ‚úì Neutral tone when presenting each view
  • ‚úì Good reasons and examples for both perspectives
  • ‚úì Clear opinion (if question asks for one)
  • ‚úì Effective use of contrast language
  • ‚úì Logical progression from view to view

How to Identify Discussion Essay Questions

üìù Discussion Essay Keywords:

Look for these phrases in the question:
  • "Discuss both views and give your opinion"
  • "Discuss both these views and give your own opinion"
  • "Some people think... while others believe... Discuss both views"
  • "Discuss both sides of this argument"
  • "There are different opinions about... Discuss"

Discussion Essay Structure

üìê Standard 4-Paragraph Structure:
Most Band 7+ discussion essays follow this proven structure:

1Ô∏è‚É£ Introduction (3-4 sentences, ~50 words)

  • Hook/Context: Introduce the general topic
  • Present both views: Briefly mention the two perspectives
  • Thesis: State that you will discuss both views (+ opinion if asked)
Example:
"The role of education has become a subject of debate. While some believe universities should focus on practical job skills, others argue for academic knowledge. This essay will discuss both perspectives before presenting my view that a balance is necessary."

2Ô∏è‚É£ Body Paragraph 1 - First View (5-7 sentences, ~90 words)

  • Topic sentence: Introduce the first viewpoint (neutral tone)
  • Explanation: Why do people hold this view?
  • Example/Evidence: Support with specific example
  • Further development: Extend the argument
  • Concluding sentence: Summarize this perspective
Language Tips:
  • Start: "On the one hand..." / "Those who support X argue that..."
  • Stay neutral: "They believe..." / "This view holds that..."
  • Use objective language throughout

3Ô∏è‚É£ Body Paragraph 2 - Second View (5-7 sentences, ~90 words)

  • Topic sentence: Introduce the opposing viewpoint (neutral tone)
  • Explanation: Why do people hold this alternative view?
  • Example/Evidence: Support with specific example
  • Further development: Extend the argument
  • Concluding sentence: Summarize this perspective
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: This paragraph should be approximately the SAME LENGTH as Body Paragraph 1. Unbalanced discussion = lower Task Response score!
Language Tips:
  • Start: "On the other hand..." / "However, others contend that..."
  • Use contrasting language: "Conversely..." / "In contrast..."
  • Maintain neutrality

4Ô∏è‚É£ Conclusion (2-3 sentences, ~40 words)

  • Summarize both views briefly: Recap the two perspectives in one sentence
  • State your opinion (if asked): Which view do you support, or do you see merit in both?
  • Final thought (optional): Broader implication or recommendation
Two Approaches:
1. Choose a side: "While both views have merit, I believe that..."
2. Balanced view: "In my opinion, the most effective approach combines elements of both perspectives..."
‚úÖ Total Word Count: 270-290 words
Introduction: ~50 | Body 1: ~90 | Body 2: ~90 | Conclusion: ~40

Alternative Structure (5 Paragraphs)

When to use 5 paragraphs:
  • If View A has two distinct arguments worth separating
  • If View B has two distinct arguments worth separating
  • If the question explicitly mentions multiple reasons for each view
Structure: Introduction + 2 paragraphs for View A + 2 paragraphs for View B + Conclusion
‚ö†Ô∏è However, 4 paragraphs is generally safer and easier to balance!

  • Count words/sentences as you write
  • In planning, write the same number of ideas for each view

üí¨ Essential Language for Discussion Essays

1. Presenting the First View

On the one hand

Formal, common opening

"On the one hand, proponents of digital learning argue that..."

Those who support X believe that

Neutral attribution

"Those who support stricter regulations believe that public safety is paramount."

Some people argue that

General, neutral

"Some people argue that remote work increases productivity."

Advocates of X claim that

For specific groups

"Advocates of renewable energy claim that fossil fuels damage the environment."

It is often argued that

Passive, formal

"It is often argued that university education should be free."

From one perspective

Alternative opening

"From one perspective, economic growth should be prioritized."

2. Transitioning to the Second View (Contrasting)

On the other hand

Most common transition

"On the other hand, critics maintain that environmental protection is more important."

However, others believe that

Clear contrast

"However, others believe that traditional methods are more effective."

Conversely

Formal opposite view

"Conversely, opponents argue that restrictions limit innovation."

In contrast

Showing difference

"In contrast, there are those who believe face-to-face interaction is irreplaceable."

Alternatively

Presenting option 2

"Alternatively, some experts suggest that prevention is better than cure."

Nevertheless

Despite previous view

"Nevertheless, many educators maintain that exams measure knowledge effectively."

3. Neutral Language (Don't Show Bias!)

Use these phrases to stay neutral when presenting views:
Neutral Phrases ‚úÖ Biased Phrases ‚ùå "They argue that..." "They wrongly claim that..." "This view holds that..." "This obviously incorrect view..." "Proponents believe..." "Some people naively think..." "It is claimed that..." "It is clearly true that..." "According to this perspective..." "Unfortunately, some believe..."

4. Giving Your Opinion (In Conclusion)

In my opinion

Direct, clear

"In my opinion, the benefits of technology outweigh traditional methods."

I believe that

Personal stance

"I believe that a balanced approach is most effective."

While both views have merit

Acknowledging both first

"While both views have merit, I lean towards the second perspective."

From my perspective

Formal opinion

"From my perspective, practical skills should take precedence."

I would argue that

Assertive but respectful

"I would argue that combining both approaches yields the best results."

It seems to me that

Softer opinion

"It seems to me that environmental concerns should be prioritized."

5. Balanced Conclusion Phrases

For a balanced/middle-ground opinion:
  • "The ideal solution lies in combining elements of both approaches..."
  • "Rather than choosing one view over the other, I believe..."
  • "Both perspectives offer valuable insights, and the best approach is..."
  • "While X has merit in certain contexts, Y is more suitable for..."
  • "A balanced approach that incorporates both views would..."

üí° Where Should You Place Your Opinion?

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL DECISION: Discussion essays have two common structures. Choose based on the question wording and your preference!

Choose Your Structure

Option 1: Opinion in Conclusion ONLY

Structure:

  • Introduction: Present both views neutrally
  • Body 1: Discuss first view (neutral)
  • Body 2: Discuss second view (neutral)
  • Conclusion: Give your opinion + summarize

‚úÖ Easiest to stay neutral

‚úÖ Clear, organized structure

‚úÖ Works for all discussion questions

‚ö†Ô∏è Less emphasis on your view

Option 2: Opinion Throughout

Structure:

  • Introduction: Present both views + state opinion
  • Body 1: Discuss first view (neutral)
  • Body 2: Discuss second view (neutral)
  • Conclusion: Restate opinion + summarize

‚úÖ Clear position from the start

‚úÖ Strong thesis statement

‚ö†Ô∏è Must still stay neutral in body paragraphs

‚ö†Ô∏è Harder to maintain balance

Option 3: Opinion in 3rd Body Paragraph

Structure:

  • Introduction: Present both views
  • Body 1: First view (neutral)
  • Body 2: Second view (neutral)
  • Body 3: Your opinion (with reasoning)
  • Conclusion: Brief summary

‚úÖ More space to develop your view

‚ö†Ô∏è 5-paragraph essay (harder to finish in time)

‚ö†Ô∏è May unbalance the discussion

üìù Your Selected Structure

Decision Flowchart

‚ùì Question says "give your opinion"

‚Üí You MUST give your opinion

Choose Option 1 or 2 (safest: Option 1)

‚ùì Question only says "discuss both views"

‚Üí Opinion is optional (but recommended)

Brief opinion in conclusion shows critical thinking!

‚ùì Not sure which to use?

‚Üí Use Option 1 (opinion in conclusion only)

This is the safest, most balanced approach for Band 7+

üéØ Examiner's Perspective:

"I don't care WHEN you give your opinion, as long as:
  • ‚úì You discuss BOTH views fairly and equally first
  • ‚úì You actually give an opinion (if the question asks for one)
  • ‚úì Your opinion is clear and supported
  • ‚úì The essay maintains logical organization
All three options can achieve Band 8-9 if executed well!"

‚≠ê Band 8 Model Essay

üìù Question:
"Some people think that parents should teach children how to be good members of society. Others believe that school is the place to learn this. Discuss both these views and give your own opinion."
üí° As you read: Click on each sentence to reveal its purpose in the essay structure!

Band 8 Discussion Essay

Introduction

The question of who should be responsible for teaching children social values has become increasingly debated in modern society.

While some argue that parents are primarily responsible for this education, others maintain that schools should play this role.

This essay will examine both perspectives before arguing that a combination of both approaches is most effective.

Body Paragraph 1 - First View (Parents' Role)

On the one hand, those who support parental responsibility argue that families provide the foundation for social development.

They believe that children learn fundamental values such as respect, honesty, and kindness primarily through observing and interacting with their parents from a very young age.

For instance, a child who witnesses their parents treating others with courtesy is more likely to internalize these behaviors than one who only encounters such lessons in a classroom setting.

Furthermore, parents have the advantage of being able to provide consistent guidance throughout daily life, reinforcing positive behaviors in real-world contexts rather than through abstract instruction.

This view holds that the intimate, ongoing nature of family relationships makes parents the most effective teachers of social conduct.

Body Paragraph 2 - Second View (Schools' Role)

On the other hand, advocates of school-based social education present equally compelling arguments.

They contend that schools provide structured environments where children can learn to interact with diverse peers and authority figures, which is essential for functioning in broader society.

For example, through group projects and classroom activities, students develop crucial skills such as cooperation, conflict resolution, and respect for different viewpoints that may not emerge within a homogeneous family unit.

Moreover, professional educators are specifically trained in child development and social pedagogy, potentially offering more systematic and informed approaches than parents who rely primarily on their own upbringing.

From this perspective, schools serve as vital training grounds for the social competencies required in modern, diverse societies.

Conclusion

While both parents and schools can effectively teach social values, each with their distinct advantages,

I believe that the most successful approach combines both influences.

Parents provide the emotional foundation and consistent modeling, while schools offer structured opportunities for practice in diverse social contexts, making collaboration between these two institutions essential for raising well-adjusted citizens.
