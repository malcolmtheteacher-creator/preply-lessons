Welcome to Lesson 4

The Power of Fillers:
In English conversations, awkward silences can make people uncomfortable. Native speakers use filler phrases to keep conversations flowing smoothly, transition between topics naturally, and give themselves time to think. These aren't "bad habits" - they're essential communication skills!

Today's Focus: Small Talk & Conversation Fillers

Native speakers rarely have perfectly smooth conversations without any pauses or transitions. Instead, they use conversation fillers to bridge gaps, change topics, and keep dialogue natural. Today you'll learn 6 essential filler phrases that will make your English conversations flow effortlessly.

Why This Matters

Being able to keep a conversation going is just as important as having good vocabulary. These filler phrases help you:
  • Buy time to think of what to say next
  • Change topics without seeming rude
  • Sound more natural and fluent
  • Prevent awkward silences
  • Show you're engaged in the conversation

Smooth Topic Transitions

Click each expression to see the meaning and example
These phrases help you change topics naturally without awkward jumps. Use them when you want to shift the conversation to something related.

3 Ways to Transition Topics

1. "Speaking of which..." / "Speaking of..."
Meaning: Connects to something just mentioned - signals you're moving to a related topic
Formality: Neutral (works everywhere)
When to use: When the current topic reminds you of something related
Example conversation:
A: "I had such a busy week at work."
B: "Yeah, I can imagine. Speaking of work, did you hear about the new office opening downtown?"
2. "That reminds me..." / "That makes me think of..."
Meaning: Shows a mental connection to something else - very natural way to switch topics
Formality: Informal/Casual
When to use: When something in the conversation triggers a memory or related thought
Example conversation:
A: "I love Italian food. That pasta was amazing."
B: "That reminds me - have you been to that new Italian place on Main Street? I heard it's fantastic."
3. "By the way..." / "Oh, by the way..."
Meaning: Introduces a new or slightly different topic - signals a shift in conversation
Formality: Neutral/Casual
When to use: When you want to mention something important you just remembered or move to a new topic
Example conversation:
A: "So that's what happened at the meeting."
B: "Interesting. By the way, did you get my email about the project deadline?"

Practice Topic Transitions

Practice flowing between these topics using transition phrases:

Scenario 1:
Someone mentions their vacation → You want to talk about travel tips
Try: "Speaking of..." or "That reminds me..."
Scenario 2:
You're discussing movies → You remember you need to ask about borrowing a book
Try: "By the way..." or "Speaking of which..."
Scenario 3:
Talking about the weather → You want to mention weekend plans
Try: "That reminds me..." or "By the way..."
Pro Tip: After using a transition phrase, briefly explain the connection:
"Speaking of work... (I wanted to mention something about my project)"
"That reminds me... (We were talking about restaurants and I just remembered)"
This makes the transition feel more natural and less abrupt.

Thinking Fillers & Pauses

It's OK to Think! Native speakers use these phrases constantly to give themselves a moment to think. They prevent awkward silences and show you're engaged in the conversation. Don't feel guilty about using them - they're natural and expected!

3 Ways to Buy Thinking Time

4. "You know what..." / "You know..."
Meaning: Emphasizes what you're about to say or gives you time to organize your thoughts
Formality: Informal/Casual
When to use: When you want to make a point or need a second to think
Example conversation:
A: "What do you think about working from home?"
B: "You know what, I actually think it depends on the person. Some people love it, others hate it."
5. "Anyway..." / "But anyway..."
Meaning: Returns to the main point or wraps up a digression - signals refocusing
Formality: Neutral
When to use: When you got sidetracked and want to return to your original point, or to move forward
Example conversation:
A: "So we went to three different stores looking for the right paint color..."
B: "Wow, that's dedication!"
A: "Yeah. Anyway, we finally found the perfect shade and finished the living room this weekend."
6. "So..." / "Well..."
Meaning: The most basic fillers - give you time to think and signal you're about to continue
Formality: Universal (works everywhere)
When to use: Anytime you need a brief pause to gather your thoughts
Example conversation:
A: "How do you feel about the new proposal?"
B: "Well... I think it has some good points, but I'm concerned about the timeline. So, my main issue is..."

The Art of Natural Pausing

Natural vs. Awkward Silence

❌ Awkward (No Fillers):
A: "What did you do this weekend?"
B: [long silence] "I went hiking."

✅ Natural (With Fillers):
A: "What did you do this weekend?"
B: "Oh, well, you know... I actually went hiking up in the mountains. It was beautiful!"

Practice Using Fillers

Try answering these questions using filler phrases:

Question 1:
"What's your opinion on electric cars?"
Try starting with: "You know what..." or "Well..."
Question 2:
Tell a story but go off on a tangent, then return to the main point
Use: "Anyway..." to get back on track
Question 3:
"Why did you choose your career?"
Use: "So..." and "Well..." while thinking
Important: These fillers work best when combined with good eye contact and an engaged tone. If you look away and mumble, they sound uncertain. If you maintain eye contact and use them confidently, they sound natural!

Key Takeaways

Remember these principles:

1. Fillers are natural and necessary: Don't feel guilty about using them. Native speakers use them constantly.

2. Transitions make you sound fluent: Smoothly moving between topics shows conversation mastery, not just vocabulary knowledge.

3. Confidence matters more than perfection: Use fillers confidently with eye contact, and they sound natural. Mumble them while looking away, and they sound uncertain.

4. Practice makes automatic: The more you use these phrases, the more naturally they'll come out in real conversations.

5. Listen to native speakers: Pay attention to how often English speakers use these exact phrases in movies, podcasts, and daily life.
