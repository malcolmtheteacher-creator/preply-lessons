Academic Writing Skills

Mastering Common Error Patterns

Level: B2-C1 Duration: 50 minutes Focus: Writing Accuracy

Articles: A, An, The, or Nothing?

Why Articles Are Tricky

Articles are one of the most common errors in academic writing. Many languages don't have articles, so students forget them or use them incorrectly.

Rule 1: Use "THE" when referring to something specific

✗ WRONG Disaster took place in 2008.
✓ CORRECT The disaster took place in 2008.

Why? We're talking about a specific disaster that was already mentioned.

✗ WRONG Scientists study climate.
✓ CORRECT Scientists study the climate.

Why? "The climate" = Earth's climate system (specific, unique thing).

Rule 2: Use "A/AN" for general, non-specific things (singular countable)

✗ WRONG She wrote essay about history.
✓ CORRECT She wrote an essay about history.

Why? "An essay" = one essay (not a specific one, just any essay).

Rule 3: NO article for general plural or uncountable nouns

✗ WRONG The scientists try to find the answers.
✓ CORRECT Scientists try to find answers.

Why? We're talking about scientists and answers in general, not specific ones.

✗ WRONG The knowledge is power.
✓ CORRECT Knowledge is power.

Why? "Knowledge" is uncountable and used generally here.

Rule 4: Special cases in academic writing

  • Use "the": the environment, the economy, the government, the solar system, the internet
  • NO article: society, technology, science, history, nature (when general)
  • Mountains: Mount Everest (no article) BUT the Himalayas (mountain range)
  • Years/dates: in 2008 (no article), in the 21st century (with "the")

Click each sentence to see the correction and explanation:

1. Research was conducted by team of scientists.
Correction: The research was conducted by a team of scientists.
Why? "The research" = specific research mentioned. "A team" = one team (singular countable).

Correction: The study examined the impact of climate change on polar bears.
Why? Specific study and specific impact. "Climate change" has no article (general concept).

Why? General historians and general sources (no article). "The past" is specific.

Why? "The internet" is always "the". "The way" = the specific manner.

Relative Pronouns: Which, That, Who, Where, When

The Problem

Using the wrong relative pronoun makes sentences confusing and grammatically incorrect. Each pronoun has specific uses!

Rule 1: WHO = people (subject or object)

✗ WRONG The scientists which conducted the study...
✓ CORRECT The scientists who conducted the study...

Why? Scientists are people, so use "who".

✗ WRONG The climbers which were trapped...
✓ CORRECT The climbers who were trapped...

Why? Climbers are people.

Rule 2: WHICH/THAT = things, animals, ideas

✗ WRONG The disaster who happened in 2008...
✓ CORRECT The disaster that happened in 2008...

Why? A disaster is not a person, so use "that" or "which".

Which vs. That:

  • THAT = defining/essential information (no comma)
  • WHICH = extra/non-essential information (with comma)
✓ WITH "THAT"

The study that was published in 2020 showed...

We need to know WHICH study - the information is essential.

✓ WITH "WHICH"

The study, which was published in 2020, showed...

Extra information - we already know which study.

Rule 3: WHERE = places

✗ WRONG The mountain which climbers died...
✓ CORRECT The mountain where climbers died...

Why? The mountain is the location/place.

Rule 4: WHEN = time

✗ WRONG The year which it happened...
✓ CORRECT The year when it happened...

Why? The year refers to time.

Quick Reference Chart

For... Use... Example
People WHO The researcher who discovered...
Things WHICH/THAT The method that scientists use...
Places WHERE The lab where they work...
Time WHEN The day when the accident occurred...
Possession WHOSE The author whose book was banned...

Prepositions: The Small Words That Matter

Why Prepositions Are Difficult

Prepositions are small but crucial. One wrong preposition can change the entire meaning of a sentence!

Common Preposition Errors in Academic Writing

✗ WRONG in terms from height
✓ CORRECT in terms of height

Why? "In terms of" is a fixed expression.

✗ WRONG the responsibility was not (incomplete!)
✓ CORRECT whose responsibility it was

Why? Need to complete the thought.

✗ WRONG focus from studying
✓ CORRECT focus on studying

Why? "Focus on" is the correct collocation.

✗ WRONG rely to evidence
✓ CORRECT rely on evidence

Why? "Rely on" is the correct collocation.

Time Prepositions

  • IN: months, years, centuries, seasons → in August, in 2008, in the 21st century, in winter
  • ON: specific dates, days → on August 1st, on Monday, on the first day
  • AT: specific times → at 3pm, at midnight, at the time

Common Academic Collocations

Verb + Preposition Example
focus on focus on the main issue
rely on rely on evidence
consist of consist of three parts
refer to refer to the data
result in result in changes
lead to lead to problems
account for account for 50%
compare to/with compare it to previous studies

Word Choice & Collocations

The Problem

Using the wrong word (even if it's close in meaning) makes your writing sound unnatural and can confuse readers.

Common Word Choice Errors

✗ WRONG a snowslide destroyed the area
✓ CORRECT an avalanche destroyed the area

Why? "Avalanche" is the correct term for snow sliding down a mountain. "Snowslide" is not standard English.

✗ WRONG high attitude
✓ CORRECT high altitude

Why? "Altitude" = height above sea level. "Attitude" = a person's way of thinking or feeling.

✗ WRONG This disaster let us to compare
✓ CORRECT This disaster allows us to compare / enables us to compare

Why? "Let" is too informal. "Allow" or "enable" are better for academic writing. Also, "allow/enable + object + TO + verb".

✗ WRONG knowledge can vary
✓ CORRECT interpretations/conclusions/findings can vary

Why? Be more specific - what exactly varies? The knowledge itself or the way we interpret it?

Academic vs. Informal Vocabulary

❌ Avoid (Too Informal) ✅ Use (Academic)
a lot of numerous, many, significant amounts of
get obtain, acquire, achieve, receive
show demonstrate, illustrate, indicate, reveal
use utilize, employ, apply, implement
big significant, substantial, considerable
good/bad positive/negative, beneficial/detrimental, effective/ineffective
thing factor, aspect, element, issue, phenomenon
anyway / basically However, Nevertheless, Fundamentally (or remove entirely)

Confusing Word Pairs

AFFECT (verb)

= to influence or change something

Climate change affects biodiversity.

EFFECT (noun)

= the result or consequence

The effects of climate change are visible.

ALTITUDE

= height above sea level

The plane reached an altitude of 10,000 meters.

ATTITUDE

= a way of thinking or feeling

Scientists have a skeptical attitude toward claims.

HISTORIC

= famous or important in history

The moon landing was a historic event.

HISTORICAL

= related to history/the past

Historical sources help us understand the past.

Sentence Structure

Common Structure Problems

Good sentence structure makes your writing clear and easy to follow. Poor structure confuses readers!

Problem 1: Run-on Sentences

✗ WRONG

The disaster occurred in 2008 when 11 climbers died on K2 which is the world's second highest mountain at 8,611 meters where an avalanche destroyed the bottleneck which led to climbers being trapped in the death zone of the mountain which is very dangerous.

✓ CORRECT

The disaster occurred in 2008, when 11 climbers died on K2, the world's second-highest mountain (8,611 meters). An avalanche destroyed the "Bottleneck" section, trapping climbers in the mountain's "Death Zone."

Why? Break long sentences into shorter ones. One idea per sentence!

Problem 2: Incomplete Sentences (Fragments)

✗ WRONG Because scientists analyzed the data carefully.
✓ CORRECT Scientists analyzed the data carefully.
OR Because scientists analyzed the data carefully, they discovered new patterns.

Why? "Because" creates a dependent clause - it needs a main clause to complete the sentence.

Problem 3: Awkward Word Order

✗ WRONG Historians should of also took facts into account.
✓ CORRECT Historians should have also taken facts into account.

Why? "Should of" is always wrong - it's "should have." Also, "took" → "taken" (past participle).

Problem 4: Missing Punctuation

✗ WRONG However science has limitations it cannot explain everything.
✓ CORRECT However, science has limitations; it cannot explain everything.
OR However, science has limitations. It cannot explain everything.

Why? Need comma after "however" and either a semicolon or period between two complete ideas.

How to Fix Long, Complex Sentences

Strategy: Break it into multiple sentences

❌ TOO COMPLEX

The disaster which took place on August 1, 2008, when 11 climbers from different groups died on K2 which is the world's second mountain in terms of height (8,611 metres) happened because a snowslide destroyed the Bottleneck area which led to climbers being trapped.

✅ CLEARER

The disaster occurred on August 1, 2008, when 11 climbers from different expeditions died on K2, the world's second-highest mountain (8,611 meters). An avalanche destroyed the "Bottleneck" section, trapping climbers in the dangerous conditions above.

Sentence Variety

Good writing uses different sentence types. Don't start every sentence the same way!

❌ Repetitive:

Scientists study climate. Scientists use data. Scientists make predictions. Scientists publish results.

✅ Varied:

Scientists study climate using various methods. They collect data, analyze patterns, and make predictions. These findings are then published in academic journals.

Academic Writing Style

What Makes Writing "Academic"?

Academic writing should be:

  • Formal - avoid casual language
  • Objective - avoid emotional language
  • Precise - use exact, specific words
  • Clear - easy to understand
  • Well-structured - logical organization

Remove Informal Words

✗ WRONG Basically, a snowslide destroyed the zone.
✓ CORRECT An avalanche destroyed the area.

Why? "Basically" is filler - it adds no meaning. Remove it for clearer writing.

✗ WRONG Anyway, this disaster let us compare it.
✓ CORRECT This disaster allows us to compare these approaches.

Why? "Anyway" is conversational. Also, be specific - "it" is vague.

Be Precise and Specific

✗ VAGUE The thing about science is that it's pretty reliable.
✓ SPECIFIC Scientific methodology provides reliable results due to empirical testing and peer review.

Why? "Thing" is vague. "Pretty" is informal. Be specific about WHAT and WHY.

Use Appropriate Hedging

Academic writing often uses "hedging" words to show that statements are probable, not absolute:

  • Too strong: Science proves that climate change is caused by humans.
  • Better: Scientific evidence suggests/indicates that human activity likely/probably contributes to climate change.

Hedging words: may, might, could, possibly, probably, likely, suggests, indicates, appears to, seems to, tends to

Style Checklist

Before you submit your essay, check:

Have I removed all informal words? (basically, anyway, a lot of, get, thing)
Are all my nouns specific and precise?
Have I used academic vocabulary instead of simple words?
Did I avoid emotional or subjective language?
Are my sentences varied in length and structure?
Have I used appropriate hedging where needed?
Is my writing clear and easy to follow?

Before and After Example

❌ INFORMAL/WEAK

Basically, science tries to figure out why things happen by getting data and doing tests. The thing is, even though science is pretty reliable, two scientists can explain the same thing differently. This shows that science can have different views too.

✅ ACADEMIC/STRONG

Natural sciences employ the scientific method to determine causal relationships through empirical data collection and experimentation. Although scientific methodology provides reliable results, interpretations may vary between researchers. This demonstrates that scientific conclusions can be influenced by different theoretical frameworks.
