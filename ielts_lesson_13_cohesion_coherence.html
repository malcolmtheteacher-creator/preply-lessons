What is Cohesion & Coherence?

Coherence & Cohesion = 25% of your IELTS Writing score

This is one of the four marking criteria. Understanding it properly can boost your score significantly!

The Difference

COHERENCE

The logical flow of ideas

  • Do ideas make sense together?
  • Is there a clear progression?
  • Can the reader follow your argument?
  • Is your essay well-organized?

COHESION

The grammatical and lexical connections

  • Reference words (this, these, such)
  • Substitution and ellipsis
  • Conjunctions and linking words
  • Lexical chains (related words)

Band 7 Requirements

Band 7: Coherence & Cohesion

What examiners look for:

  • Logically organizes information and ideas; clear progression throughout
  • Uses a range of cohesive devices appropriately (not mechanically)
  • Presents a clear central topic within each paragraph
  • May have occasional lapses in coherence or inappropriate cohesion

The Four Cohesive Devices

Device What It Does Example
Reference Points back to previously mentioned items The government introduced new policies. These measures proved effective.
Substitution Replaces words to avoid repetition Students study hard. Teachers do so as well.
Ellipsis Omits words that are understood Some people support the plan; others [do] not.
Conjunction Connects ideas and shows relationships Technology helps learning. However, it also creates distractions.
Common Mistake: Using too many linking words (like "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally") in every sentence. Simon (Band 9 examiner) says this actually CAPS your score at Band 6 because it sounds mechanical!
The Secret: Band 8-9 essays use cohesion SO naturally that you don't notice it. The focus is on IDEAS, not on fancy connecting words.

Mastering Reference Words

Reference = Pointing back to something already mentioned

This creates connection between sentences and avoids repetition.

Types of Reference

Personal Reference

Pronouns: it, they, he, she, them, their

Many teenagers use smartphones daily. They rely on them for communication.

Demonstrative Reference

This, that, these, those, such

Online education has become popular. This trend will continue growing.

Comparative Reference

Same, similar, different, other, another

Some prefer face-to-face learning. Others favor online courses.

Reference Chains in Academic Writing

Example: Building a Reference Chain

Sentence 1: The government introduced new environmental regulations in 2020.
Sentence 2: These policies aimed to reduce carbon emissions.
Sentence 3: Such measures have proven effective in other countries.
Sentence 4: However, they remain controversial among business owners.
Analysis: Notice how we refer to the same concept (environmental regulations) in four different ways:
  • new environmental regulations (original)
  • These policies (demonstrative + synonym)
  • Such measures (demonstrative + synonym)
  • they (pronoun)
This creates NATURAL cohesion without repetition!

Common Reference Words for IELTS

Function Reference Words Example
Pointing to ideas this, that, these, those Technology improves efficiency. This advantage is significant.
Referring to situations such, this situation, this problem Many students struggle financially. Such difficulties affect their studies.
Referring to people they, them, their, those who Young people use social media. They spend hours online.
Referring to things it, this, that, these The policy was controversial. It faced strong opposition.

References:
- "This problem" refers back to "Environmental pollution"

- "this habit" refers to "spending excessive time on social media"

References:
- "it" refers to "Online education"
- "This disadvantage" refers to the lack of personal interaction
Common Mistake: Making unclear references
"Governments should tax unhealthy food and use the money to promote exercise. This would improve public health."

Problem: Does "this" refer to taxing food OR promoting exercise OR both?
"Governments should tax unhealthy food and use the money to promote exercise. This combined approach would improve public health."

Better: Clear reference to BOTH measures

Substitution & Ellipsis

Substitution = Replacing words with other words
Ellipsis = Leaving out words that are understood

Both techniques help avoid repetition and create natural flow.

Substitution Types

Nominal Substitution

one, ones, the same

Some people prefer traditional education; others choose online ones.

Verbal Substitution

do, do so, does

Teachers work hard. Students should do so as well.

Clausal Substitution

so, not

"Will technology replace teachers?" I think not.

Substitution Examples in IELTS Writing

Example 1: Using "one/ones"

Without substitution (repetitive):
Some students prefer face-to-face classes, while other students prefer online classes.
With substitution (better):
Some students prefer face-to-face classes, while others prefer online ones.

Example 2: Using "do so"

Without substitution (repetitive):
Governments should invest in renewable energy. Private companies should invest in renewable energy too.
With substitution (better):
Governments should invest in renewable energy. Private companies should do so too.

Ellipsis in Academic Writing

Ellipsis = Omitting words that can be understood from context

This is common in comparisons and parallel structures.

Example 1: Verb Ellipsis

Full version: Some people support the policy, and others oppose the policy.
With ellipsis: Some people support the policy, and others [do] not.

The verb "oppose" is understood from context

Example 2: Noun Ellipsis

Full version: Rich countries produce more waste than poor countries produce waste.
With ellipsis: Rich countries produce more waste than poor [countries] [do].

Both the noun "countries" and the verb "produce" are understood

Improved: Young people enjoy traveling. Old people do so as well.
Technique: Verbal substitution with "do so"

Paragraph Linking Techniques

Good paragraph linking = Clear progression from one idea to the next

Examiners specifically look for "clear progression throughout" for Band 7+

Three Ways to Link Paragraphs

1. Topic Sentences

Start each paragraph with a clear topic sentence that connects to the previous idea

Para 1: ...benefits of exercise.
Para 2: Besides physical benefits, exercise also improves mental health.

2. Transitional Words

Use appropriate signposting words (but don't overuse!)

Para 1: First reason...
Para 2: Furthermore, second reason...

3. Reference Back

Refer back to ideas from the previous paragraph

Para 1: ...technology in education.
Para 2: While these technological advances offer benefits...

Analysis: Well-Linked Essay

INTRODUCTION:
Many people believe that university education should be free for all students. While this idea has merit, I believe that charging reasonable fees is more sustainable and fair.
BODY PARAGRAPH 1:
Firstly, free university education would create an enormous financial burden on governments. Most countries already struggle with budget deficits, and adding the cost of higher education would worsen this problem. For example, the UK government spends billions on education already. Such expenses would increase dramatically if all university fees were eliminated.
BODY PARAGRAPH 2:
In addition to financial concerns, charging moderate fees ensures that students value their education. When people pay for something, they tend to take it more seriously. Research shows that students who pay tuition fees have higher completion rates than those who do not. This commitment to finishing their studies benefits both the individual and society.
CONCLUSION:
In conclusion, while free university education may seem appealing, the financial and practical challenges make it unrealistic. The current system of moderate fees, combined with scholarships for needy students, offers a better balance.
What makes this well-linked?
  • Clear topic sentences starting each paragraph
  • Appropriate transitional phrases (Firstly, In addition to)
  • Reference words connecting ideas (this problem, such expenses, they, this commitment)
  • Logical progression: Introduction → Problem 1 → Problem 2 → Conclusion
  • Conclusion references back to "current system" mentioned earlier

Common Paragraph Transitions

Function Transitions Example
Adding ideas Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, Besides this Body 1 discusses benefits. Furthermore, Body 2 discusses more advantages.
Contrasting ideas However, On the other hand, Nevertheless, Conversely Body 1 shows advantages. However, Body 2 examines disadvantages.
Showing result Therefore, Consequently, As a result, Thus Problems discussed. Therefore, solutions are needed.
Sequencing Firstly, Secondly, Finally, To begin with Firstly, reason one. Secondly, reason two.
Simon's Warning: Don't start EVERY paragraph with "Furthermore," "Moreover," or "In addition." This sounds mechanical. Sometimes a simple topic sentence without a transition word is better!
Mechanical (Band 6):
Para 1: Firstly, technology helps learning...
Para 2: Secondly, technology improves communication...
Para 3: Thirdly, technology creates opportunities...

Natural (Band 7-8):
Para 1: Technology has transformed education in recent years...
Para 2: Beyond educational benefits, technology also enables global communication...
Para 3: Perhaps the most significant impact is in creating new career opportunities...

Analyze Cohesion in Real Essays

Learning from examples: The best way to understand cohesion is to see it in action. Below are two essays on the same topic - one with poor cohesion (Band 5-6) and one with excellent cohesion (Band 7-8).

Topic

Question: Some people believe that unpaid community service should be a compulsory part of high school programs. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

Essay A: Poor Cohesion (Band 5-6)

Community service is good for students. Students learn many things. It helps the community.

Students can learn responsibility. They can help old people. They can clean parks. This is important.

Some people think community service takes too much time. Students are busy. They have homework. They have exams.

I think community service should be required. It is good for students. It is good for society. Students should do community service.

Problems with Essay A:
  • Very short, repetitive sentences
  • No clear reference words (keeps repeating "students," "community service")
  • No paragraph linking
  • Ideas feel disconnected and choppy
  • Mechanical structure, no flow

Essay B: Excellent Cohesion (Band 7-8)

Community service has become an increasingly important component of modern education. While some argue that making it compulsory places undue pressure on students, I firmly believe that mandatory community work offers significant benefits to both young people and society.

Firstly, engaging in unpaid community work helps teenagers develop essential life skills and character. When young people volunteer at local charities, care homes, or environmental projects, they learn valuable qualities such as empathy, responsibility, and teamwork. For instance, a teenager volunteering at a food bank not only assists those in need but also gains a deeper understanding of social inequality. This hands-on experience is often more impactful than classroom learning alone.

Furthermore, community service programs address genuine social needs while strengthening community bonds. Many organizations, particularly in areas such as elderly care and youth mentoring, rely heavily on volunteers to function effectively. By requiring teenagers to participate, communities benefit from increased support for these vital services. Moreover, when young people work alongside adults from diverse backgrounds, they develop a stronger sense of civic responsibility that extends into adulthood.

Admittedly, critics argue that mandating service work infringes on teenagers' already limited free time, which could be spent on academic studies or extracurricular activities. However, this concern overlooks the fact that community service itself is an educational experience that can complement formal schooling. Indeed, many universities now value volunteer experience as highly as academic achievements when making admissions decisions.

In conclusion, I strongly believe that requiring teenagers to participate in community service creates a win-win situation for both young people and society. The personal development and civic awareness gained through such programs far outweigh concerns about time commitment.

Why Essay B has excellent cohesion:
  • Reference words: it, they, this hands-on experience, these vital services, this concern, such programs
  • Substitution: "such as" used naturally instead of repeating "like"
  • Clear transitions: Firstly, Furthermore, Admittedly, However, Indeed, In conclusion
  • Paragraph linking: Each paragraph flows naturally from the previous one
  • Lexical chains: community service → it → mandatory community work → such programs
  • Natural flow: Ideas connect smoothly without being mechanical

Your Turn: Cohesion Checklist

When writing your own essays, check for these cohesive devices:
Device Self-Check Question
Reference Words Have I used "this," "these," "such," "it," "they" to avoid repetition?
Substitution Have I used "ones," "do so," "the same" where appropriate?
Transitions Are my paragraphs linked with appropriate transition words?
Variety Have I varied my sentence structures and avoided repetition?
Natural Flow Does my essay read smoothly, or does it sound mechanical?
Final Reminder from Simon (IELTS Examiner):

"Band 9 essays use cohesion in such a way that it attracts no attention. The focus should be on your IDEAS and VOCABULARY, not on linking words. Natural cohesion happens when you think clearly about your argument and express it logically."
