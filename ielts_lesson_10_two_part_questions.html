Understanding Two-Part Questions

🎯 What Are Two-Part Questions?

Two-part questions contain two distinct questions that must each be answered fully and equally. These are among the most challenging IELTS Task 2 questions because students must:

  • Identify and understand both questions
  • Address BOTH parts fully and equally
  • Organize their essay clearly
  • Use appropriate language for each part

📊 Example Two-Part Question:

"In many countries, the number of people choosing to live alone is increasing."

Part 1: Why is this happening?
Part 2: Is this a positive or negative development for society?

🔑 Common Two-Part Combinations:

1. Why? + Positive/Negative?

Explain causes/reasons + Give evaluation

2. Problems? + Solutions?

Identify problems + Propose solutions

3. Causes? + Effects?

Explain causes + Describe effects

4. Why? + Who is responsible?

Explain reasons + Assign responsibility

⚠️ Critical Rule for Two-Part Questions:

TWO distinct questions = TWO separate body paragraphs

Each body paragraph should address ONE part of the question only. Never mix both parts in one paragraph!

💡 Pro Tips for Identification:

  • "Why?" or "What are the reasons?" → Needs causal language (because, due to, stems from)
  • "What problems/effects?" → Needs problem/effect language (consequences, results in, leads to)
  • "What solutions?" → Needs solution language (could be addressed by, measures to tackle)
  • "Positive or negative?" → Needs evaluative language (beneficial, detrimental, advantageous)
  • "Who is responsible?" → Needs responsibility language (bear responsibility, incumbent upon)

Essay Structure for Two-Part Questions

📝 Recommended Structure

The clearest and highest-scoring structure for two-part questions is:

INTRODUCTION (3-4 sentences)

  • Hook/General statement about the topic
  • Paraphrase BOTH questions
  • Thesis statement answering BOTH parts briefly

BODY PARAGRAPH 1 - Answers Part 1 (120-150 words)

  • Topic sentence clearly addressing Part 1
  • Main idea 1 + explanation + example
  • Main idea 2 + explanation + example
  • (Optional: Main idea 3)
  • Link sentence

BODY PARAGRAPH 2 - Answers Part 2 (120-150 words)

  • Topic sentence clearly addressing Part 2
  • Main idea 1 + explanation + example
  • Main idea 2 + explanation + example
  • (Optional: Main idea 3)
  • Link sentence

CONCLUSION (2-3 sentences)

  • Restate answer to Part 1 briefly
  • Restate answer to Part 2 briefly
  • Final thought (optional)

If one body paragraph is significantly longer or more developed than the other, your Task Response score will drop to Band 5-6, even if your English is excellent!

Target: Both body paragraphs should be approximately equal length (within 20 words of each other).

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Learn from these common errors that students make with two-part questions.

❌ Mistake #1: Imbalanced Response

The Problem: One part gets much more attention than the other.

Example:

Body 1 (Why people live alone): 180 words - fully developed with 3 reasons
Body 2 (Positive or negative?): 60 words - only 1 weak point

Impact: Task Response drops to Band 5-6

Solution: Plan equal development for both parts. Check word count as you write.

❌ Mistake #2: Merged Response

The Problem: Both parts addressed in one paragraph instead of separate paragraphs.

Example:

Body Paragraph: "This is happening because of X, Y, and Z. This is negative because of A, B, and C."

Impact: Coherence & Cohesion drops to Band 5-6

Solution: Always use separate paragraphs for each part. One paragraph = one part.

❌ Mistake #3: Missing Part

The Problem: One part is barely addressed or not addressed at all.

Example:

Introduction: mentions both parts
Body 1: Why people live alone
Body 2: More reasons why people live alone
Conclusion: Briefly mentions "it's negative" but never developed in body

Impact: Task Response drops to Band 4-5

Solution: During planning, specifically note which body paragraph addresses which part.

❌ Mistake #4: Unclear Topic Sentences

The Problem: Reader can't tell which part each paragraph addresses.

Weak Example:
"There are several factors to consider." (Which part are you answering?)

Strong Example:
"There are several reasons why more people are choosing to live alone." (Clearly Part 1)

Solution: Use clear topic sentences that explicitly signal which part you're addressing.

✅ How to Avoid These Mistakes:

  1. Plan carefully: Spend 8-10 minutes planning before writing
  2. Label your plan: Mark which paragraph answers which part
  3. Check during writing: Pause after Body 1 to ensure you're on track
  4. Count words: Aim for similar length in both body paragraphs
  5. Review before finishing: Read through and verify both parts are fully addressed

Model Essay Analysis

Study this Band 8 model essay. Click on paragraphs to see how each part is addressed.

Question: "In many countries, the number of people choosing to live alone is increasing. Why is this happening? Is this a positive or negative development for society?"

Band 8 Model Essay

Introduction

Solo living has become an increasingly common lifestyle choice in many developed nations.

This essay will examine the factors driving this trend and argue that it represents a largely positive development for modern society.

Body Paragraph 1 - Part 1: Why?

There are several key reasons why more people are opting to live alone.

Firstly, improved economic conditions and higher incomes have made solo living financially viable for a larger proportion of the population. Whereas previous generations often had to share housing due to financial constraints, many young professionals today can afford to rent or purchase their own accommodation.

Secondly, changing social attitudes have reduced the stigma traditionally associated with living alone. In the past, solo dwellers were often viewed as isolated or unable to form relationships, but modern society increasingly values independence and personal space.

For instance, in countries like Sweden and Japan, solo living is now seen as a sign of self-sufficiency rather than social failure.

Body Paragraph 2 - Part 2: Positive or Negative?

I believe this trend toward solo living should be viewed as predominantly positive for several reasons.

From an individual perspective, living alone offers significant benefits in terms of personal freedom and self-development. People can establish their own routines, make independent decisions about their living environment, and enjoy complete privacy. This autonomy can lead to increased confidence and self-reliance.

Furthermore, the rise in single-person households has positive economic implications. It drives demand for smaller, more affordable housing units and stimulates various service industries, from food delivery to entertainment streaming services, thereby creating employment opportunities.

While some argue that solo living can lead to social isolation, modern technology enables people to maintain strong social connections despite living alone.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the increase in people living alone stems from both economic prosperity and evolving social values, and this development brings more advantages than disadvantages to contemporary society.

As individualism continues to be valued in modern culture, solo living is likely to remain a popular and valid lifestyle choice.

[Word count: 286 words]

🎯 Why This is Band 8:

  • Task Response: Both parts fully and equally addressed. Clear position throughout. Well-developed ideas with examples.
  • Coherence & Cohesion: Perfect organization with separate paragraphs for each part. Excellent use of linking (Firstly, Secondly, Furthermore, While). Clear progression of ideas.
  • Lexical Resource: Sophisticated vocabulary (viable, stigma, self-sufficiency, autonomy, implications). Natural collocations. Precise word choice.
  • Grammar: Wide variety of complex structures. Conditional clauses, relative clauses, subordination. Virtually error-free.
