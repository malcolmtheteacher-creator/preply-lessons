4️⃣ ADJECTIVES
Definition: An adjective describes or modifies a noun or pronoun. It tells us WHICH, WHAT KIND, or HOW MANY.
The tall student read a fascinating book about ancient history in the quiet library.

Each adjective adds more information about the noun it describes.

Types of Adjectives

1. Descriptive Adjectives

Describe qualities or characteristics

Size: big, small, huge, tiny
Color: red, blue, golden, dark
Shape: round, square, triangular
Age: young, old, new, ancient
Opinion: beautiful, ugly, interesting, boring
Feeling: happy, sad, excited, tired

2. Quantitative Adjectives

Tell us HOW MANY or HOW MUCH

Numbers: one, two, three, first, second
Quantity: some, many, few, several, much, little

She has three books and many friends.

3. Demonstrative Adjectives

Point to specific nouns

This book is mine. That car is expensive.
These students are brilliant. Those problems are difficult.

4. Possessive Adjectives

Show ownership

My book, your pen, his car, her house, its color, our team, their ideas

5. Order of Adjectives

When using multiple adjectives, there's a specific order:

Order Type Examples
1 Opinion beautiful, ugly, lovely
2 Size big, small, huge
3 Age old, young, new
4 Shape round, square, flat
5 Color red, blue, green
6 Origin French, German, Chinese
7 Material wooden, metal, plastic
8 Purpose sleeping (bag), running (shoes)
✓ Correct: A beautiful big old round red French wooden dining table.
✗ Wrong: A wooden French red round old big beautiful dining table.

Memory trick: "OSASCOMP" - Opinion, Size, Age, Shape, Color, Origin, Material, Purpose

6. Comparative and Superlative Forms

Base Form Comparative (than) Superlative (the)
tall taller tallest
happy happier happiest
beautiful more beautiful most beautiful
good better best
bad worse worst

Rule: Short adjectives (1-2 syllables) add -er/-est. Long adjectives (3+ syllables) use more/most.

💡 Quick Test: How to identify adjectives?
  • Does it describe a noun? (beautiful house, fast car)
  • Can you make it comparative? (fast → faster → fastest)
  • Can you put "very" in front of it? (very tall, very interesting)
5️⃣ ADVERBS
Definition: An adverb modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb. It tells us HOW, WHEN, WHERE, HOW OFTEN, or TO WHAT EXTENT.
She quickly finished the extremely difficult test yesterday and performed remarkably well.

• quickly = modifies verb "finished" (HOW she finished)
• extremely = modifies adjective "difficult" (HOW MUCH difficult)
• yesterday = modifies verb "finished" (WHEN she finished)
• remarkably = modifies adverb "well" (TO WHAT EXTENT well)

Types of Adverbs

1. Adverbs of Manner (HOW?)

Describe how an action is performed

She speaks fluently. He drives carefully. They worked hard.
Common: quickly, slowly, carefully, badly, well, hard, fast

Formation: Most adverbs of manner = adjective + "-ly" (quick → quickly, careful → carefully)

2. Adverbs of Time (WHEN?)

Tell us when something happens

I saw him yesterday. She will call tomorrow. They arrived early.
Common: now, today, yesterday, tomorrow, soon, later, already, yet, still

3. Adverbs of Place (WHERE?)

Tell us where something happens

Come here. Look everywhere. They went outside.
Common: here, there, everywhere, nowhere, outside, inside, upstairs, downstairs

4. Adverbs of Frequency (HOW OFTEN?)

Tell us how often something happens

I always study. She usually arrives early. They never complain.
Common: always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, never, seldom

Position: Usually BEFORE the main verb, but AFTER "be"

✓ I often go. / ✓ She is often late. / ✗ I go often.

5. Adverbs of Degree (HOW MUCH?)

Tell us the intensity or degree

It's very hot. She's extremely intelligent. I completely agree.
Common: very, too, quite, rather, extremely, absolutely, completely, almost, nearly

6. Adjective vs Adverb

Adjective (describes noun) Adverb (describes verb/adj/adv)
She is a quick learner. She learns quickly.
He is a careful driver. He drives carefully.
It's a good book. She writes well. (NOT "goodly"!)
He is a hard worker. He works hard. (NOT "hardly"!)
"Hardly" means "almost not"
⚠️ Common Mistakes:
  • good (adjective) vs well (adverb): "He plays good" ✗ → "He plays well" ✓
  • hard (adverb) vs hardly (= almost not): "He works hard" ✓ / "He hardly works" = He almost doesn't work
  • late (adverb) vs lately (= recently): "He arrived late" ✓ / "Lately, I've been busy" ✓
  • near (adverb) vs nearly (= almost): "Come near" ✓ / "It's nearly finished" ✓
🧠 Memory Trick: Most adverbs end in -ly, but not all! Some common exceptions: fast, hard, well, late, early, far, near
6️⃣ PREPOSITIONS
Definition: A preposition shows the relationship between a noun/pronoun and another word in the sentence. It shows TIME, PLACE, DIRECTION, or other relationships.
The book on the table beside the window was written by an author from Ukraine. I found it during my visit to the library.

4. Other Important Prepositions

Cause/Reason: because of, due to, thanks to
Purpose: for
Agent: by (passive voice: "written by Shakespeare")
Possession: of ("the color of the sky")
Accompaniment: with, without
Manner: with, by ("by car," "with care")

5. Prepositional Phrases

Preposition + noun/pronoun = prepositional phrase

The book on the table is mine.
He arrived at midnight.
She lives in Berlin.
They traveled by train.
⚠️ Common Mistakes for Ukrainian/German Speakers:
  • Dependent on (NOT: "dependent from")
  • Different from/to (NOT: "different as" - German influence)
  • Interested in (NOT: "interested for")
  • Afraid of (NOT: "afraid from")
  • At the weekend (British) / On the weekend (American)
  • In the street (British) / On the street (American)
🧠 Memory Trick: Prepositions often describe where a squirrel can be in relation to a tree: in the tree, on the tree, under the tree, beside the tree, near the tree, above the tree, etc.
7️⃣ CONJUNCTIONS
Definition: A conjunction connects words, phrases, or clauses.

Types of Conjunctions

1. Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS)

Connect words, phrases, or independent clauses of equal importance

For - reason/explanation
And - addition
Nor - negative addition
But - contrast
Or - alternative/choice
Yet - contrast
So - result/consequence
I studied hard, and I passed the exam.
She is tired, but she keeps working.
Do you want tea or coffee?
He didn't study, so he failed the test.

2. Subordinating Conjunctions

Connect a dependent clause to an independent clause

Type Conjunctions Example
Time when, while, before, after, since, until, as soon as I'll call you when I arrive.
Cause/Reason because, since, as I stayed home because I was sick.
Condition if, unless, provided that I'll go if you come with me.
Contrast although, though, even though, whereas, while Although I was tired, I finished my work.
Purpose so that, in order that I studied hard so that I could pass.
Result so...that, such...that It was so cold that the lake froze.

3. Correlative Conjunctions

Work in pairs to connect equal elements

both...and → Both Maria and John are students.
either...or → You can have either tea or coffee.
neither...nor → Neither Tom nor Sarah came to the party.
not only...but also → She is not only intelligent but also hardworking.
whether...or → I don't know whether to stay or leave.

Important: The structure after both parts must be parallel!

✓ She is both intelligent and beautiful. (both adjectives)
✗ She is both intelligent and has beauty. (not parallel)

4. Compound Sentences with Conjunctions

Pattern: Independent Clause + Conjunction + Independent Clause

I wanted to go to the cinema, but I had no money.
She studied all night, so she was very tired.
He likes coffee, and she prefers tea.

Punctuation: Use a comma before coordinating conjunctions when connecting independent clauses!

5. Complex Sentences with Subordinating Conjunctions

Pattern 1: Independent Clause + Subordinating Conjunction + Dependent Clause

I will stay home because I am sick. (NO comma)

Pattern 2: Subordinating Conjunction + Dependent Clause + Independent Clause

Because I am sick, I will stay home. (comma needed!)
⚠️ Common Mistakes:
  • "Because" cannot start an answer to "Why?"
    Q: Why are you late? A: "Because I missed the bus." ✗
    Correct: "I missed the bus." ✓ OR "It's because I missed the bus." ✓
  • "So" and "because" CANNOT be used together
    "Because I was tired so I went to bed." ✗
    "I was tired, so I went to bed." ✓ OR "Because I was tired, I went to bed." ✓
  • Don't confuse "while" (time) with "but" (contrast)
8️⃣ INTERJECTIONS
Definition: An interjection is a word or phrase that expresses sudden emotion. It stands alone, grammatically independent from the rest of the sentence.
Wow! That's amazing!
Ouch! That hurt!
Oh no, I forgot my keys!
Hooray! We won!

Common Interjections

Joy/Excitement
Wow!, Yay!, Hooray!, Yippee!, Woohoo!, Awesome!
Surprise
Oh!, Wow!, Gosh!, Whoa!, Really?, Seriously?
Pain/Distress
Ouch!, Ow!, Help!, Oh no!, Yikes!
Disgust
Ew!, Yuck!, Gross!, Ugh!
Hesitation
Um..., Uh..., Er..., Well...
Agreement
Yes!, Yeah!, Okay!, Alright!, Sure!
Greeting
Hi!, Hello!, Hey!, Bye!, Goodbye!
Attention
Hey!, Look!, Listen!, Psst!

Punctuation with Interjections

Strong Emotion Mild Emotion
Use exclamation mark (!) Use comma (,)
Wow! That's incredible! Well, I think so.
Ouch! That hurt! Oh, I see.
Help! I'm stuck! Um, I don't know.
💡 Important Notes:
  • Interjections are common in spoken English but less common in formal writing
  • They don't grammatically connect to the rest of the sentence
  • Be careful not to overuse them in academic writing!
  • Different cultures use different interjections - these are English ones
🧠 Why are interjections easy? They're the simplest part of speech! They don't change form, don't connect to other words, and you already use them naturally when you speak emotionally. Just remember: save them for informal communication!
