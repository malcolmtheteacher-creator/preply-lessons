Why This Lesson Matters:
In English, saying "you should" repeatedly sounds repetitive and sometimes pushy. Native speakers use a variety of expressions to give advice diplomatically, ranging from gentle suggestions to direct recommendations. Today you'll master 6 natural ways to give advice.

Today's Focus: Giving Advice & Suggestions

There's an art to giving advice in English. Too direct and you sound bossy. Too soft and people don't take you seriously. Today you'll learn 6 expressions for giving advice at different levels of directness, then practice using them with real-life problem scenarios.

Gentle Advice & Suggestions

Click each expression to see the meaning and example
These expressions are softer and more diplomatic. Use them when you want to suggest something without being pushy.

3 Gentle Ways to Give Advice

1. "Have you thought about..." / "Have you considered..."
Meaning: Very gentle suggestion - you're asking if they've considered an option
Formality: Neutral (works in most situations)
Strength: Very gentle - leaves them room to say no
When to use: When you want to plant an idea without pushing
Example conversation:
A: "I'm exhausted all the time and I don't know why."
B: "Have you thought about getting your vitamin levels checked? That helped me when I felt the same way."
2. "Why don't you..."
Meaning: Friendly, informal suggestion - sounds like a casual idea
Formality: Informal/Casual
Strength: Gentle - friendly and conversational
When to use: With friends, family, or people you know well
Example conversation:
A: "I hate my commute. It takes two hours each day."
B: "Why don't you look into remote work options? A lot of companies are offering that now."
3. "It might be worth..." / "It could be worth..."
Meaning: Diplomatic suggestion - you're saying something could have value
Formality: Neutral/Slightly formal
Strength: Moderate - suggests benefit without pressure
When to use: Professional settings or when you want to sound thoughtful
Example conversation:
A: "I keep forgetting important dates and deadlines."
B: "It might be worth setting up a digital calendar with reminders. I found it really helps with organization."

Practice Using Gentle Advice

Try responding to these problems using gentle advice expressions:

Problem 1:
"I want to learn guitar but I don't have time to take lessons."
Try: "Have you thought about..." or "Why don't you..."
Problem 2:
"My back hurts every day from sitting at my desk."

Pro Tip: Gentle advice works best when you add your own experience:
"Have you thought about [advice]? That's what worked for me when..."
"It might be worth [advice]. I've heard good things about it."

Direct Advice (But Still Polite)

When to Be More Direct: Sometimes people need clear advice, not just gentle suggestions. These expressions are stronger but still polite. Use them when someone asks directly for your advice or when the situation is serious.

3 Direct Ways to Give Advice

4. "If I were you, I'd..." / "If I were in your shoes, I'd..."
Meaning: Classic advice phrase - you're saying what you would do in their situation
Formality: Neutral
Strength: Moderately strong - clear recommendation
When to use: When someone asks "What would you do?" or when you have relevant experience
Example conversation:
A: "Should I accept the job offer? The salary is lower but the work is more interesting."
B: "If I were you, I'd take it. Money isn't everything, and you'll be happier doing work you enjoy."
5. "You could always..."
Meaning: Presenting an option they might not have thought of - sounds flexible
Formality: Neutral/Informal
Strength: Moderate - offers a clear option without forcing it
When to use: When suggesting an alternative or backup plan
Example conversation:
A: "I want to travel but I don't have enough vacation days."
B: "You could always take unpaid leave if your company allows it, or look for a job with better vacation benefits."
6. "My advice would be to..." / "I'd recommend..."
Meaning: Direct, authoritative advice - you're stating your clear recommendation
Formality: Formal/Professional
Strength: Strong - this is your definite opinion
When to use: When someone specifically asks for your advice, or in professional contexts
Example conversation:
A: "What should I do about my difficult coworker who keeps taking credit for my work?"
B: "My advice would be to document everything and schedule a meeting with your manager. This needs to be addressed formally."

The Complete Advice Formula

Step 1: Show empathy/understanding
"I understand that's difficult..." / "That's a tough situation..." / "I see why you're concerned..."

Step 2: Give your advice using one of the expressions
"If I were you, I'd..." / "My advice would be to..." / "You could always..."

Step 3: Explain why (give a reason or benefit)
"...because..." / "The reason I say that is..." / "That way you'll..."

Optional Step 4: Offer support or alternatives
"But ultimately, it's your decision." / "Of course, you know your situation best." / "Another option could be..."

Practice Giving Direct Advice

Try responding to these problems using direct advice expressions:

Problem 1:
"My roommate never cleans and I'm getting really frustrated. What should I do?"
Try: "If I were you, I'd..." or "My advice would be to..."
Problem 2:
"I failed my driving test twice. Should I give up?"
Try: "You could always..." or "If I were in your shoes, I'd..."
Problem 3:
"My company wants me to relocate but my family doesn't want to move."
Try: "My advice would be to..." or "If I were you, I'd..."
Important: Even with direct advice, respect their autonomy. You can end with phrases like:
• "But it's really up to you."
• "That's just my opinion."
• "You know your situation better than I do."

Key Takeaways

Remember these principles:

1. Match advice strength to the situation: Use gentle advice for sensitive topics; use direct advice when someone specifically asks for your opinion.

2. Always explain your reasoning: Don't just say "do this" - explain WHY you think it's a good idea.

3. Show empathy first: Start with "I understand..." or "That's difficult..." before giving advice.

4. Respect their autonomy: End with "But it's your decision" to show you're not being controlling.

5. Use your own experience: Advice is more powerful when you say "This worked for me because..."
