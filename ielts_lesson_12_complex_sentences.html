Why Complex Sentences Matter for IELTS

Grammar Range & Accuracy (GRA) Criterion:
To achieve Band 7+, you need to demonstrate a "variety of complex structures" with "frequent error-free sentences." Complex sentences are essential for this score.

What Are Complex Sentences?

Complex sentences contain one independent clause (complete sentence) and one or more dependent clauses (incomplete ideas that depend on the main clause).

Simple Sentence

Community service helps teenagers.

One independent clause - Band 5-6

Compound Sentence

Community service helps teenagers, and it benefits local communities.

Two independent clauses - Band 6

Complex Sentence

Community service, which involves unpaid work, helps teenagers develop essential life skills.

Independent + dependent clause - Band 7+

Three Main Types of Complex Sentences for IELTS

Type Purpose Example
Relative Clauses Add extra information about nouns Students who volunteer regularly develop strong work ethics.
Conditionals Express hypothetical situations If governments invested more, education would improve.
Passive Voice Focus on action/result, not doer Traditional skills are being replaced by technology.
Important: Complex sentences must be ACCURATE. One error in a complex structure hurts your score more than a simple sentence done perfectly. Always prioritize accuracy over complexity!

How Many Complex Sentences Do I Need?

For Band 7: Aim for 50-60% complex sentences (about 7-10 in a 270-word essay)
For Band 8: Aim for 70%+ complex sentences (about 12-15 in a 270-word essay)
Mix them throughout your essay - don't cluster them all in one paragraph!

Relative Clauses in Academic Writing

Relative clauses add essential or extra information about nouns. They begin with relative pronouns: who, which, that, where, whose

1. Defining Relative Clauses (Essential Information)

These clauses are necessary to identify which person/thing we're talking about. NO commas are used.

Correct Examples:
- Students who study regularly tend to perform better in exams.
- The benefits that community service provides are substantial.
- Countries where education is prioritized show higher economic growth.

2. Non-Defining Relative Clauses (Extra Information)

These clauses add bonus information that could be removed. USE commas to separate them.

Correct Examples:
- Technology, which has revolutionized communication, affects every aspect of modern life.
- My hometown, where I spent my childhood, has changed dramatically.
- Online education, which became popular during COVID-19, offers flexibility.
Common Mistake: Using "that" in non-defining clauses
Wrong: Technology, that has revolutionized communication, affects modern life.
Right: Technology, which has revolutionized communication, affects modern life.

3. When to Use Each Relative Pronoun

Pronoun Use For Example
who People Teachers who inspire students make a lasting impact.
which Things/Animals Skills which are learned early become automatic.
that People/Things (defining only) The approach that works best depends on context.
where Places Cities where pollution is high face health challenges.
whose Possession Students whose parents are supportive often succeed.

Conditional Structures for IELTS Writing

Conditionals express hypothetical situations and are particularly useful for problem-solution essays and when proposing solutions or discussing possibilities.

1. First Conditional - Real Future Possibilities

Form: If + present simple, will + base verb

Use: Things that are likely to happen

Examples:
- If governments invest in education, future generations will benefit.
- Students will perform better if they receive adequate support.
- If this trend continues, environmental problems will worsen.

2. Second Conditional - Unreal Present/Future

Form: If + past simple, would + base verb

Use: Imaginary or unlikely situations in present/future

Examples:
- If tuition fees were lower, more students would attend university.
- We would see improvements if governments prioritized healthcare.
- If people had more leisure time, they would pursue hobbies.
IELTS Tip: The second conditional is perfect for proposing solutions! "If governments implemented X, the situation would improve."

3. Third Conditional - Unreal Past

Form: If + past perfect, would have + past participle

Use: Imaginary situations in the past (less common in IELTS)

Examples:
- If earlier generations had protected the environment, we would not face these problems today.
- Education systems would have evolved differently if technology had been available sooner.

4. Mixed Conditionals

Combine different times - past action with present result

Examples:
- If governments had invested in infrastructure, cities would be better equipped today.
- If we had addressed climate change earlier, the situation would not be so critical now.

5. Zero Conditional - General Truths

Form: If + present simple, present simple

Use: Scientific facts or things that are always true

Examples:
- If students receive encouragement, they perform better.
- If people exercise regularly, they experience health benefits.

Alternative Forms (More Advanced)

Instead of starting with "If," use these for variety:

Advanced Structures:
- Were governments to invest in education, results would improve. (formal inversion)
- Should funding increase, more programs could be implemented.
- Had previous policies been effective, we would not face these challenges.
- Unless action is taken, the problem will worsen. (= If action is not taken)

Passive Voice in Academic Writing

The passive voice is common in academic writing when the action or result is more important than who does it. However, don't overuse it - IELTS values natural, varied writing.

When to Use Passive Voice

1. Action is More Important

Traditional skills are being replaced by modern technology.

(Focus on what's happening, not who's doing it)

2. Doer is Unknown/Unimportant

The policy was implemented in 2015.

(We don't know or care who implemented it)

3. Doer is Obvious

English is spoken in many countries worldwide.

(Obviously by people - no need to state)

Passive Voice Forms by Tense

Tense Active Passive
Present Simple Technology affects education. Education is affected by technology.
Present Continuous Technology is changing education. Education is being changed by technology.
Present Perfect Technology has transformed education. Education has been transformed by technology.
Past Simple The government introduced new policies. New policies were introduced by the government.
Future Simple AI will replace many jobs. Many jobs will be replaced by AI.
Modal Verbs We should address this issue. This issue should be addressed.

Common IELTS Passive Structures

Useful Passive Patterns for IELTS:

Problem Statements:
- This problem is caused by several factors.
- Environmental damage is being accelerated by human activities.
- Traditional values are being eroded by modern lifestyles.

Solutions:
- This issue could be addressed through education.
- Resources should be allocated more effectively.
- Measures need to be taken to prevent further damage.

General Statements:
- It is widely believed that education is important.
- It has been argued that technology improves quality of life.
- It is generally accepted that exercise benefits health.
Balance is Key: Aim for 20-30% passive voice in your essay. Too much passive makes writing boring and unclear. Too little passive misses an opportunity to show grammatical range.

Active vs. Passive - Which is Better?

For each scenario, choose the better version:

1. Discussing a problem in your essay:

People cause air pollution in cities.
Air pollution in cities is caused by several factors.

2. Expressing your opinion:

I believe governments should prioritize education.
It is believed by me that education should be prioritized by governments.

Common Grammar Errors to Avoid

Even advanced students make these mistakes. Learn to spot and correct them!

Error 1: Missing Commas in Non-Defining Relative Clauses

Wrong: Technology which has changed our lives rapidly continues to evolve.
Correct: Technology, which has changed our lives rapidly, continues to evolve.

If the clause adds extra information (could be removed), use commas!

Error 2: Using "That" in Non-Defining Relative Clauses

Wrong: Social media, that was invented in the 2000s, has transformed communication.
Correct: Social media, which was invented in the 2000s, has transformed communication.

Never use "that" with commas. Use "which" for non-defining clauses.

Error 3: Wrong Conditional Forms

Wrong: If governments will invest in education, the economy would improve.
Correct: If governments invest in education, the economy will improve. (First conditional)

Don't use "will" in the if-clause of first conditionals!

Error 4: Passive Voice with Wrong Form

Wrong: Many problems can be solve through education.
Correct: Many problems can be solved through education.

Passive = be + past participle (solved, not solve)

Error 5: Subject-Verb Agreement in Relative Clauses

Wrong: Students who studies hard usually succeed.
Correct: Students who study hard usually succeed.

The verb agrees with "students" (plural), not "who"!

Error 6: Overusing Passive Voice

Wrong: It is believed by many people that education is important. Traditional methods are being used by teachers. Students are taught by educators who are qualified.
Correct: Many people believe that education is important. Teachers use traditional methods. Qualified educators teach students.

Too much passive makes writing boring! Mix active and passive naturally.

Change "solve" to "solved"
Change "can be" to "can"
