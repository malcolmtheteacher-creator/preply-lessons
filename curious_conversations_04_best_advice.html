Advice You Ignored at 20 But Understand at 30

"We're all drowning in advice.
The trick is knowing which advice to keep."

Everyone gives advice. Your parents, teachers, friends, random people on the internet. Some of it's terrible. Some of it's brilliant. And sometimesâ€”the worst partâ€”you get brilliant advice and completely ignore it because you're not ready to hear it yet.

It took me years to appreciate the advice my parents gave me about money. "Save 10% of every paycheck," they said. "Start now, even if it's just a little." I thought they were being boring and overly cautious. Now? I wish I'd started a decade earlier.

Warren Buffett's "Two-List" Technique

Warren Buffett's pilot once asked him for career advice. Buffett told him: "Write down your top 25 goals. Circle your top 5. Now avoid the other 20 at all costsâ€”they're distractions."

The turning point was when I actually tried this. I realized I was spending time on things that didn't matter. Focusing on fewer goals changed everything.

"Done Is Better Than Perfect"

A teacher once told me: "You're waiting for perfect conditions that will never come. Just start. Done is better than perfect." In hindsight, I should have listened years earlier. I wasted so much time planning instead of doing.

"You Can't Control What Others Think"

My grandmother used to say: "Worrying about what others think is a waste of your precious time." I thought she didn't understand social pressure. But I've come to realize that she was rightâ€”the people whose opinions actually matter don't judge you harshly, and the people who judge you harshly don't matter.

Counterintuitive Advice That Turned Out True:

"Slow down to go faster"

Taking breaks and resting actually makes you more productive. Burnout slows you down more than rest ever will.

"Say no more often"

Every "yes" to something unimportant is a "no" to something that matters. Protecting your time is protecting your life.

"Fail faster"

The faster you fail, the faster you learn. Waiting for perfect conditions means you learn nothing.

"Do it badly first"

Start with a terrible first draft. You can't edit a blank page, but you can always improve something that exists.

Talking About Hindsight & Reflection

You just read about advice that took time to appreciate. Let's learn how to talk about looking back on your decisions and realizing things you didn't see before.

Click each card to reveal the meaning and examples. Then try using it yourself!

1. "It took me years to appreciate..."
Formality: Neutral
Meaning: You didn't understand or value something when you first encountered it, but with time and experience, you finally understood its worth or truth. Implies growth, maturity, or changed perspective over time.
Example:
"It took me years to appreciate my parents' advice about saving money. When I was 20, I thought they were just being paranoid. Now at 35, I'm grateful I eventually listened."
NOW YOU TRY: What took YOU years to appreciate? (Could be advice, a person, an experience, a skill...)
2. "In hindsight, I should have..."
Formality: Neutral
Meaning: Looking back with the benefit of knowing what happened, you now realize what you should have done differently. "Hindsight" means understanding something only after it happened. Often expresses regret or a lesson learned.
Example:
"In hindsight, I should have learned a second language when I was younger. My brain was more flexible then, and I had more free time. Now it's much harder."
NOW YOU TRY: What do you think, in hindsight, you should have done differently? (Not for traumaâ€”just decisions you'd change if you could go back)

Expressing Realizations & Growth

Now let's look at how to talk about the moments when things finally make senseâ€”turning points and realizations.

3. "The turning point was when..."
Formality: Neutral
Meaning: The specific moment or event that changed everythingâ€”when your direction, understanding, or situation shifted. A "turning point" is when things go from one state to another. Often marks the beginning of significant change.
Example:
"I struggled with English for years, but the turning point was when I started watching TV shows without subtitles. Suddenly I was learning naturally instead of just memorizing grammar rules."
NOW YOU TRY: What was a turning point in your life? When did something finally click or change for you?
4. "I've come to realize that..."
Formality: Neutral to Formal
Meaning: Through experience and time, you've gradually reached an understanding or conclusion. "Come to realize" suggests a slow process, not a sudden revelation. Implies maturity, reflection, or accumulated wisdom.
Example:
"I've come to realize that the best relationships aren't the ones without conflictâ€”they're the ones where people know how to resolve conflict well. That understanding changed how I approach disagreements."
NOW YOU TRY: What have you come to realize over time? What do you understand now that you didn't before?
5. "That advice has served me well"
Formality: Neutral to Formal
Meaning: That advice has been useful and beneficial over time; it's helped you make good decisions or avoid problems. "Served me well" means it's been valuable and worth following. Positive and grateful tone.
Example:
"My teacher told me to always read the entire question before answering on exams. That advice has served me wellâ€”not just in tests, but in life. Now I listen fully before responding to anything."
NOW YOU TRY: What advice has served YOU well over the years? What guidance did you follow that turned out to be valuable?
