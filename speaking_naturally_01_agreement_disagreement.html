Welcome to Speaking Naturally

About This Series:
Speaking Naturally is a 6-part series designed to help you sound more natural and confident in English conversations. Each lesson focuses on a specific aspect of spoken English with plenty of practice time.

Today's Focus: Agreement & Disagreement

In English conversations, there are dozens of ways to agree or disagree beyond "I agree" and "I disagree." Today you'll learn 5 natural ways to agree and 5 natural ways to disagree politely, then practice using them in real conversations.

What You'll Learn Today

  • 5 expressions for strong agreement
  • 5 expressions for polite disagreement
  • How to sound diplomatic when you disagree
  • When to use formal vs. informal expressions
  • Practice with controversial topics (your choice!)
  • Timed debates to build fluency

Why This Matters

Using varied expressions makes you sound more fluent and natural. Native speakers rarely say "I agree" or "I disagree" directly - they use more sophisticated phrases that show the strength of their opinion and maintain good relationships even when they disagree.

Agreeing Naturally

5 Ways to Agree in English

1. "Absolutely" / "Absolutely right"
Meaning: Strong agreement - you completely agree
Formality: Neutral (works in most situations)
When to use: When someone says something obviously true or you strongly agree
Example conversation:
A: "Learning a language takes time and practice."
B: "Absolutely. There's no shortcut to fluency."
2. "I couldn't agree more"
Meaning: Maximum agreement - it's impossible to agree more than you do
Formality: Slightly formal
When to use: When you want to emphasize your agreement
Example conversation:
A: "We need better public transportation in this city."
B: "I couldn't agree more. The current system is a nightmare."
3. "Exactly" / "Exactly right"
Meaning: You agree completely and the person expressed it perfectly
Formality: Neutral/Informal
When to use: When someone says precisely what you were thinking
Example conversation:
A: "The problem isn't the technology, it's how people use it."
B: "Exactly! You've hit the nail on the head."
4. "That's a good point" / "Fair point"
Meaning: You agree with what they said and acknowledge it's intelligent/valid
Formality: Neutral
When to use: When someone makes an argument you hadn't considered
Example conversation:
A: "Remote work saves commute time, but it can be isolating."
B: "That's a good point. I hadn't thought about the social aspect."
5. "I'm with you on that"
Meaning: Informal agreement - you're on the same side/team
Formality: Informal/Casual
When to use: With friends, colleagues you know well, casual situations
Example conversation:
A: "I think we should take a break. We've been working for three hours."
B: "I'm with you on that. Let's grab a coffee."

Disagreeing Politely

The Secret to Polite Disagreement: Start with something positive or acknowledge their point, THEN present your different view. This keeps the conversation friendly.

5 Ways to Disagree Politely in English

1. "I see what you mean, but..."
Meaning: You understand their point, but you have a different opinion
Formality: Neutral
When to use: When you want to disagree diplomatically
Example conversation:
A: "Everyone should learn to code. It's essential these days."
B: "I see what you mean, but I think communication skills are equally important."
2. "I'm not sure I agree with you on that"
Meaning: Soft disagreement - you're uncertain but leaning toward disagreement
Formality: Polite/Formal
When to use: Professional settings, when you want to be extra polite
Example conversation:
A: "We should cut the budget for training programs."
B: "I'm not sure I agree with you on that. Training is a long-term investment."
3. "That's one way to look at it, but..."
Meaning: You acknowledge their viewpoint is valid, but you have a different perspective
Formality: Neutral
When to use: When there are multiple valid perspectives on an issue
Example conversation:
A: "Social media is destroying real communication."
B: "That's one way to look at it, but it's also connecting people across the world."
4. "I hear you, but have you considered..."
Meaning: You acknowledge what they said, then introduce a different angle
Formality: Informal/Neutral
When to use: When you want to add information they may not have thought about
Example conversation:
A: "Electric cars are the solution to pollution."
B: "I hear you, but have you considered where the electricity comes from?"
5. "Actually, I think..." / "To be honest, I think..."
Meaning: Direct but polite disagreement - you're stating your real opinion
Formality: Neutral
When to use: When you want to be clear about your different viewpoint
Example conversation:
A: "Working from home makes people less productive."
B: "To be honest, I think it depends on the person. I'm actually more productive at home."

The Disagreement Formula

Step 1: Acknowledge their point
"I see what you mean..." / "That's interesting..." / "I understand your point..."

Step 2: Use a disagreement expression
"...but I'm not sure I agree because..." / "...but I think differently..."

Step 3: Give your reason
"In my experience..." / "From what I've seen..." / "I believe that..."

Practice Responses

Try disagreeing politely with these statements:

Statement 1:
"Money can't buy happiness."
Try: "I hear you, but have you considered..." or "That's one way to look at it, but..."
Statement 2:
"University education is a waste of time. You can learn everything online."
Try: "I see what you mean, but..." or "I'm not sure I agree with you on that"
Statement 3:
"People today are more stressed than previous generations."
Try: "Actually, I think..." or "That's one way to look at it, but..."
Important: Body language and tone matter! Even with polite phrases, you can sound aggressive if your tone is wrong. Keep your voice calm and friendly when disagreeing.
