The 4 Marking Criteria Explained

Your IELTS Writing Task 2 is scored based on 4 equal criteria. Understanding these is the KEY to improving your score!

‚ö° Critical Insight: Each criterion is worth 25% of your final score. You need to score well in ALL FOUR areas, not just one or two. This is why understanding the criteria is more important than memorizing templates!

Task Response (TR)

25%

What it measures: How well you answer the question

  • Clear position throughout
  • All parts of question addressed
  • Ideas fully developed
  • Relevant examples provided

Coherence & Cohesion (CC)

25%

What it measures: How well your ideas connect

  • Clear progression of ideas
  • Appropriate use of linking words
  • Well-organized paragraphs
  • Logical structure

Lexical Resource (LR)

25%

What it measures: Your vocabulary range and accuracy

  • Wide range of vocabulary
  • Natural collocations
  • Precise word choice
  • Minimal vocabulary errors

Grammatical Range & Accuracy (GRA)

25%

What it measures: Your grammar variety and control

  • Variety of sentence structures
  • Mix of simple and complex sentences
  • Accurate grammar
  • Error-free sentences
üí° Common Mistake: Many students focus only on vocabulary and grammar (LR and GRA), but ignore Task Response and Coherence. This is why they get stuck at Band 6.0! You must score well in ALL FOUR criteria to get Band 7+.

Task Response (TR) - 25% of Your Score

This criterion measures how well you answer the question. It's often where students lose the most marks!

What Examiners Look For:

  • Clear position: Your opinion must be clear from the introduction and maintained throughout
  • All parts addressed: If the question has 2 parts, you must answer BOTH equally
  • Fully developed ideas: Don't just list points - explain them with examples and details
  • Relevant content: Stay on topic! Going off-topic = lower score
  • Appropriate length: Write 250+ words (aim for 270-290)

Band Score Differences for Task Response:

Band 7
Position clear throughout; main ideas extended and supported; all parts of the task addressed with relevant ideas.

‚ùå Why Students Fail Task Response:

  1. Not analyzing the question properly - They miss key parts or misunderstand what's being asked
  2. Unclear position - The examiner can't tell what the student actually thinks
  3. Underdeveloped ideas - Short paragraphs with no examples or explanation (biggest problem!)
  4. Going off-topic - Writing about related but irrelevant information
  5. Only answering part of the question - Ignoring one of the two parts in a two-part question

Example: Good vs. Bad Task Response

Question: "Some people think that universities should provide graduates with knowledge and skills for the workplace. Others think that the true function of a university is to give access to knowledge for its own sake. Discuss both views and give your own opinion."

Response A:
"I think universities are important for education. Students learn many subjects and this helps them in life. Teachers are very experienced and share their knowledge. Education makes people smarter and helps society develop."

Coherence & Cohesion (CC) - 25% of Your Score

This measures how well your ideas flow together and how easy your essay is to read.

üîë Key Concept: Coherence = Ideas make sense and connect logically. Cohesion = Using linking words and reference words to connect sentences.

What Examiners Look For:

  • Clear progression: Ideas flow naturally from one to the next
  • Logical organization: Each paragraph has one clear main idea
  • Appropriate linking: Use linking words naturally (not mechanically)
  • Good paragraphing: Introduction, 2-3 body paragraphs, conclusion
  • Reference words: Use "this," "these," "such," etc. to connect ideas

Band Score Differences for Coherence & Cohesion:

Band 7
Clear progression throughout; range of cohesive devices used appropriately; clear central topic in each paragraph.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Coherence & Cohesion Mistakes:

  1. Overusing linking words - "Furthermore," "Moreover," "In addition" in every sentence = Band 6 maximum! (This is what Simon, the former IELTS examiner, says)
  2. Poor paragraphing - All ideas in one long paragraph, or 5+ short paragraphs
  3. No clear topic sentence - Readers don't know what each paragraph is about
  4. Ideas that jump around - Mentioning topic A, then B, then A again
  5. Mechanical linking - Using "Firstly, Secondly, Finally" but ideas don't connect logically

Lexical Resource (LR) - 25% of Your Score

This measures your vocabulary range, precision, and natural usage.

What Examiners Look For:

  • Wide range of vocabulary: Use varied words, not just basic ones
  • Natural collocations: Words that naturally go together (e.g., "make a decision," not "do a decision")
  • Precise word choice: Use the exact word that fits the meaning
  • Topic-specific vocabulary: Show you know words related to the topic
  • Paraphrasing: Don't repeat the same words from the question
  • Few errors: Small mistakes are OK at Band 7-8, but don't impede meaning

Band Score Differences for Lexical Resource:

Band 7
Flexible use of vocabulary with some precision; less common words with some awareness of style and collocation; occasional errors in word choice don't impede meaning.

‚ùå Lexical Resource Mistakes That Cost Marks:

  1. Basic vocabulary only - Using only common words like "good," "bad," "important"
  2. Repeating the same words - Using "important" 10 times instead of varying it
  3. Unnatural collocations - "do a mistake" (wrong) vs. "make a mistake" (correct)
  4. Wrong word choice - Using a word that doesn't fit the context
  5. Memorized phrases that don't fit - Examiners can spot this immediately!
  6. Copying question words - Not paraphrasing the question in the introduction

Example: Band 6 vs. Band 8 Vocabulary

Band 6 Vocabulary

"Technology is very important in modern life. Many people use smartphones every day. This is good because it helps them stay connected. However, some people think it is bad because they spend too much time on their phones."

Issues: Basic words (important, good, bad), repetitive, no sophisticated vocabulary

Band 8 Vocabulary

"Technology has become integral to contemporary existence. Widespread adoption of smartphones has transformed daily communication. This development offers considerable advantages in maintaining social connections, though critics argue that excessive screen time may have detrimental effects."

Strengths: Sophisticated vocabulary, natural collocations, precise word choice, paraphrasing

Grammatical Range & Accuracy (GRA) - 25% of Your Score

This measures the variety and accuracy of your grammar structures.

What Examiners Look For:

  • Range of structures: Mix simple, compound, and complex sentences
  • Complex sentences: Use relative clauses, conditionals, passive voice appropriately
  • Accurate grammar: Most sentences should be error-free
  • Variety: Don't use the same sentence pattern repeatedly
  • Punctuation: Correct use of commas, periods, etc.
  • Error frequency: Some minor errors are OK, but not in every sentence

Band Score Differences for Grammar:

Band 7
Variety of complex structures used; frequent error-free sentences; good control of grammar and punctuation, though some errors remain.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Grammar Insights:

  1. Complex sentences MUST be accurate - An error in a complex sentence is WORSE than a correct simple sentence
  2. Don't overdo it - You don't need complex sentences in every sentence. Mix them naturally.
  3. Common structures that impress:
    • Relative clauses: "Technology, which has transformed modern life, continues to evolve"
    • Conditionals: "If governments invested more in education, societies would benefit"
    • Passive voice: "These problems can be addressed through policy changes"
    • Participle clauses: "Having considered both views, I believe..."

Example: Simple vs. Complex Sentences

Band 6 Grammar (Simple Only)

"Education is important. It helps people find jobs. Companies need skilled workers. Universities teach these skills. Students benefit from this education. Society also benefits."

Issue: All simple sentences, no variety, repetitive structure

Band 8 Grammar (Good Mix)

"Education plays a crucial role in career development. Because companies increasingly demand specialized skills, universities must adapt their curricula accordingly. Students who acquire these competencies not only enhance their employability but also contribute to broader societal progress."

Strengths: Mix of simple and complex, subordinate clauses, correlative conjunctions (not only...but also)

Real Examples: Band 6 vs. Band 8 Essays

See the difference between a Band 6 and Band 8 response to the same question.

Question: "Some people think that governments should spend money on faster means of public transportation. Others think that there are other important priorities. Discuss both views and give your opinion."

Band 6 Response (First Paragraph)

"Nowadays, transportation is very important in our life. Some people think governments should spend money on public transportation to make it faster. But other people think there are other important things. In my opinion, I think both are important and I will discuss both views in this essay."

Analysis:

  • TR: Band 6 - Position unclear ("both are important" is not a clear stance)
  • CC: Band 6 - Basic linking only ("but," "and")
  • LR: Band 6 - Repetitive vocabulary, basic words
  • GRA: Band 6 - Simple sentence structures only

Band 8 Response (First Paragraph)

"The allocation of government funding remains a contentious issue, particularly regarding whether to prioritize enhanced public transportation systems or address alternative pressing needs. While rapid transit infrastructure offers considerable benefits, I believe that a balanced approach, which also addresses healthcare and education, ultimately serves societies more effectively."

Analysis:

  • TR: Band 8 - Clear position stated immediately with nuance
  • CC: Band 8 - Natural progression of ideas, sophisticated linking
  • LR: Band 8 - Advanced vocabulary (contentious, allocation, infrastructure), paraphrasing
  • GRA: Band 8 - Complex structures with relative clauses
