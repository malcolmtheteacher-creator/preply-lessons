Why We're So Passionate About Food

Research shows food is deeply connected to identity, memory, and culture. When someone says "your food is weird," your brain hears "your culture is weird, your family is weird, YOU are weird." That's why people are strangely passionate about what seems like trivial food choices.

The Science of "Disgusting" Foods

H√°karl (fermented shark) is an acquired taste in Iceland but makes most foreigners gag. Century eggs are a Chinese delicacy but look terrifying to outsiders. Vegemite is beloved in Australia but considered inedible elsewhere.

The science? It's sacrilege in my culture works both ways‚Äîwhat's sacred to one culture is shocking to another. And neither side is wrong.

Food Rules That Sound Insane to Outsiders

Italy: Cappuccino only before 11am. Ordering it after lunch marks you as a tourist.

France: Putting butter AND cheese on the same bread? Barbaric.

Japan: Pouring soy sauce directly on rice is deeply disrespectful to the chef.

India: Eating with your left hand is considered unclean and rude.

UK: The correct way to make tea has caused genuine arguments. Milk first or milk after? Wars have been fought over less.

Expressing Food Opinions

You just read about passionate food debates. Let's learn how to talk about strong food opinions and cultural food rules.

Click each card to reveal the meaning and examples. Then try using it yourself!

1. "That's sacrilege in my culture..."
Formality: Informal to Neutral
Meaning: "Sacrilege" means disrespecting something sacred or holy. Used (often humorously) to say something is deeply offensive to your cultural values or traditions. Shows the action is seriously wrong in your culture, even if you're being playful about it.
Example:
"Putting ketchup on good steak? That's sacrilege in my culture. My grandfather would disown you. In Argentina, we take our beef very seriously‚Äîyou don't mask the flavor with sauce."
NOW YOU TRY: What food behavior would be considered sacrilege in YOUR culture? What do people from your country get passionate about?
2. "I can't wrap my head around..."
Formality: Informal
Meaning: You can't understand or comprehend something; it's too strange or illogical for you to grasp. "Wrap my head around" suggests trying to mentally contain or understand something that's too big or weird to process. Very common in casual English.
Example:
"I can't wrap my head around eating insects. I know they're sustainable and nutritious, and millions of people eat them, but... I just can't imagine putting a cricket in my mouth. It's a mental block."
NOW YOU TRY: What food practice can't YOU wrap your head around? What seems completely illogical to you even though you know others do it?

Talking About Strange Foods & Strong Opinions

Now let's look at how to discuss foods that take time to appreciate, passionate opinions, and encouraging others to try new things.

3. "It's an acquired taste"
Formality: Neutral
Meaning: Something you need to try multiple times before you appreciate it; not enjoyable at first but becomes good with repeated exposure. Often used to politely say "most people don't like this initially." Can apply to food, music, art, or anything that grows on you over time.
Example:
"Black coffee is definitely an acquired taste. When I first tried it, I thought it was bitter and awful. But after drinking it for a few weeks, I started appreciating the complex flavors. Now I can't stand sweet coffee."
NOW YOU TRY: What food is an acquired taste that you've learned to love? What took you time to appreciate?
4. "People are strangely passionate about..."
Formality: Neutral
Meaning: People care intensely about something that seems trivial or unimportant to outsiders. "Strangely passionate" suggests the level of emotion is surprising or disproportionate. Often used when observing intense debates about minor things.
Example:
"People are strangely passionate about how to pronounce 'gif.' It's just an image format, but I've seen actual arguments break out. Same with pineapple on pizza‚Äîthe intensity of opinions is wild for something so subjective."
NOW YOU TRY: What are people strangely passionate about in your experience? What debates seem disproportionately intense?
5. "You haven't lived until you've tried..."
Formality: Informal
Meaning: Enthusiastically recommending an experience as essential; suggesting someone's life is incomplete without this experience. Obviously hyperbolic‚Äîthey have "lived"‚Äîbut emphasizes how strongly you recommend it. Very common for food, travel, and experiences.
Example:
"You haven't lived until you've tried authentic Neapolitan pizza in Naples. I know that sounds dramatic, but the difference between that and regular pizza is mind-blowing. The crust, the sauce, the buffalo mozzarella‚Äîit's transcendent."
NOW YOU TRY: What food experience do you think people "haven't lived" without trying? What would you passionately recommend?

Final Thought:

Food debates are silly. They're also deeply meaningful. They're about identity, memory, culture, and belonging. When someone says your favorite food is weird, they're (unintentionally) saying something about you. And when you can't wrap your head around someone else's food choices, you're learning where your cultural boundaries are.

So yes, fight about pineapple on pizza. Defend ketchup on eggs. Argue about the proper way to eat sushi. It's not really about the food‚Äîit's about who we are.
