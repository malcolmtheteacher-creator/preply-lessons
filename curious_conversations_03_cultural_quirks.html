Why Do Americans Smile at Strangers?

Every culture does things that seem "normal"
Until you see them from the outside...

When tourists visit America, one of the first things they notice is the smiling. Americans smile at strangers on the street, in elevators, at the grocery store. They ask "How are you?" to people they don't know and don't really want an answer from.

To Americans, this is ingrained in their culture‚Äîit's just being polite and friendly. But to many Europeans, Asians, and people from other cultures, it's completely alien to them. In some countries, smiling at strangers is seen as bizarre, fake, or even suspicious.

"Why are you smiling at me? Do I know you?" is a common reaction from people visiting from Russia, Finland, or many Asian countries.

Cultural Quirks Around the World:

Japanese Gift Wrapping

In Japan, how you wrap a gift is as important as what's inside. People spend 20 minutes carefully folding corners, tying perfect bows, and selecting the right paper colors. We take it for granted that wrapping paper is decorative, but in Japan, sloppy wrapping is seen as deeply disrespectful.

Nordic Silence

In Finland and Norway, comfortable silence is normal. Sitting with someone for 10 minutes without speaking isn't awkward‚Äîit's second nature to them. But Americans often find this intensely uncomfortable and feel the need to fill every silence with words.

British Queueing

The British are famous for their orderly queues (lines). Cutting in line is seen as one of the worst social violations. This behavior is so ingrained that British people will form a queue even when it's not strictly necessary. To outsiders, this obsession seems excessive, but to Brits, it's just a cultural thing.

American Small Talk

Americans talk to everyone‚Äîthe cashier, the person next to them on the plane, people in elevators. "Nice weather today!" "Where are you from?" "Have a great day!" This constant chatter is completely alien to many cultures where talking to strangers is unusual or even rude.

Talking About Cultural Habits

You just read about cultural quirks around the world. Let's learn how to talk about what's normal in your culture versus what seems strange from the outside.

Click each card to reveal the meaning and examples. Then try using it yourself!

1. "It's ingrained in our culture..."
Formality: Neutral to Formal
Meaning: Something is so deeply embedded in your culture that people don't even think about it‚Äîit's automatic, passed down through generations. "Ingrained" means embedded so deeply it's hard to change or even notice.
Example:
"Respecting elders is ingrained in our culture. We don't even question it‚Äîyou just automatically stand up when older people enter a room, let them speak first, and listen to their advice."
NOW YOU TRY: What's ingrained in YOUR culture that you didn't realize was culturally specific until you met people from other countries?
2. "It's completely alien to me that..."
Formality: Neutral
Meaning: Something is so foreign or strange that you can't understand it at all‚Äîit feels like it's from another planet. "Alien" emphasizes how unfamiliar and strange something feels. Can express surprise, confusion, or fascination.
Example:
"It's completely alien to me that in some cultures, people eat with their hands. I grew up using chopsticks for everything, so eating rice with your fingers just feels... wrong to me, even though I know it's normal elsewhere."
NOW YOU TRY: What cultural practice from another country is completely alien to you? What did foreigners find alien about YOUR culture?

What's Normal, What's Strange

Now let's look at how to talk about what we assume is universal‚Äîand what's actually just cultural.

3. "We take it for granted that..."
Formality: Neutral
Meaning: We assume something is true or normal without questioning it; we don't appreciate or notice it because it's always been there. Often used when realizing something you thought was universal is actually specific to your situation.
Example:
"We take it for granted that everyone shakes hands when meeting. But in my friend's culture, touching strangers‚Äîeven shaking hands‚Äîis quite uncomfortable. I never thought about how physical that greeting actually is."
NOW YOU TRY: What do people in your culture take for granted that isn't universal? What did YOU used to take for granted before traveling or meeting people from other cultures?
4. "It's second nature to..."
Formality: Neutral
Meaning: Something you do automatically without thinking because you've done it so many times‚Äîit's become instinctive. Like a "second" instinct, beyond your first natural instincts. Implies deep familiarity and ease.
Example:
"Taking off your shoes when entering a house is second nature to me. I don't even think about it‚Äîmy hand just automatically reaches for my shoes at the door. But my American friends have to consciously remember to do it at my place."
NOW YOU TRY: What's second nature to you that you've had to consciously think about when visiting other countries? What do you do automatically that confuses foreigners?
5. "That's just a cultural thing"
Formality: Informal to Neutral
Meaning: A casual way to explain that something is specific to a particular culture, not universal. Often used to acknowledge differences without judging them. Can excuse unusual behavior or explain confusion. Neutral and accepting tone.
Example:
"My coworker from Brazil stands really close when talking‚Äîat first it made me uncomfortable, but then I realized that's just a cultural thing. Personal space is different there. Now I'm used to it."
NOW YOU TRY: What have you learned is "just a cultural thing" that initially confused or surprised you? When have you explained your own behavior by saying "that's just a cultural thing"?

Using These Beyond Culture:

These expressions work for ANY situation involving habits, assumptions, and automatic behavior:

  • "We take it for granted that everyone has internet access"

Final Thought:
