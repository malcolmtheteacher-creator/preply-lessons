Teacher Conversations 01

Why I Quit Teaching

For Teachers | C1-C2 Level | 50 minutes

1 Read Together

Instructions: Teacher reads first (student listens for pronunciation). Then student reads aloud (teacher notes pronunciation issues).
"Why I Quit Teaching After 15 Years"
by Sarah Mitchell, Former High School English Teacher

I handed in my resignation on a Tuesday in November. My principal stared at me like I'd lost my mind. Maybe I had. It wasn't one big thing – it was everything. The parent who screamed at me because her son failed, even though he hadn't turned in work for three months. The administration adding another meeting when I was already drowning. The new curriculum with no training, no resources, no time. But mostly? It was realizing I couldn't remember the last time I'd actually enjoyed teaching. I used to love walking into my classroom. Now I was just surviving, counting days until Friday. The breaking point was a faculty meeting where we celebrated test scores improving by 2%. Two percent. And everyone clapped. I sat there thinking: "Is THIS what we're celebrating? Not the student who learned to love reading – just numbers?" That afternoon, I started looking for other jobs. People ask, "How could you quit mid-year?" But I can't give students my best when I'm running on empty. My last day is December 15th. I don't have another job lined up. But I have something I haven't had in years: hope.

3 Pronunciation Practice

Instructions: Focus on emotional delivery. These sentences have strong feelings – the pronunciation must show that. Teacher models first with exaggerated emotion, then student practices.

Sentence 1: Sadness & Realization

"I couldn't remember the last time I'd enjoyed teaching."
I couldn't remember the last time I'd enjoyed teaching.
How to say it:
• Slow pace (sadness slows you down)
• Falling intonation on "enjoyed teaching" (disappointment)
• Almost a sigh in the delivery
• Voice gets quieter at the end

Practice: Say it 3 times with emotion. Record yourself. Compare to teacher's model.

Sentence 2: Frustration & Disbelief

"Is THIS what we're celebrating? Numbers?"
Is THIS what we're celebrating? NUMBERS?
How to say it:
• Rising intonation on "THIS" (incredulous)
• Sharp, disgusted tone on "NUMBERS"
• Pause before "Numbers" for emphasis
• Voice expresses contempt

Practice: Say it like you can't believe what you're hearing. Make it sound ridiculous.

5 The Big Questions

Instructions: Choose 1-2 questions for mature, peer-level discussion. No time limit. Just natural conversation between two educators.
Question 1: Do you think teaching is getting worse? Or was it always this hard and we just didn't know?
Question 2: If you could change ONE thing about your school system tomorrow – no limits, no bureaucracy – what would you change?
Question 3: Sarah mentions "running on empty" and not being able to give her best. Is it better to quit when you can't give 100%, or stay because students need ANY teacher?
Question 4: What would Sarah need to hear or experience to come back to teaching? Could anything bring her back?
Question 5: When you're 60 years old and looking back, will you regret staying in teaching? Or regret if you leave?

6 The Other Side

Instructions: Read this contrasting story together. Then discuss: which teacher do you relate to more?
"Why I'm Still Teaching After 20 Years"
by Michael Chen, High School Math Teacher

People ask why I haven't quit. The money's terrible, the parents are exhausting, and the administration treats us like we're the problem. But last week, a former student messaged me. She's a doctor now. She said, "Remember when you told me I was smart enough for medical school? I didn't believe you. But you believed it enough for both of us." I barely remember that conversation. It was probably just office hours, nothing special. But to her? It changed everything. That's why I stay. Because you never know which small moment saves someone. You never know which student needs exactly what you said that day. So yeah, I'm tired. Yeah, I'm underpaid. But I'm not leaving. Not yet. Because maybe tomorrow I'll say the thing that changes someone's life. And I'd hate to miss that.

Which teacher do you relate to more?
Sarah (who quit) or Michael (who stays)? Why?
Have you ever had a "Michael moment"?
A former student who came back and told you that you made a difference?
Is Michael fooling himself?
Or is this a healthy reason to stay in teaching despite the problems?
What would it take for Michael to quit?
If this is what keeps him going, what would break him?

Pronunciation Practice: Hope & Purpose

"Maybe tomorrow I'll say the thing that changes someone's life."
Maybe tomorrow I'll say the thing that changes someone's life.
How to say it:
• Rising intonation (hope, possibility)
• Emphasis on "tomorrow" (optimism)
• Stress on "changes someone's life" (purpose)
• Slightly faster pace than sadness (energy of hope)

Practice: Compare this hopeful tone to Sarah's defeated tone from earlier.

+ Extension Activities

Use these if you have extra time (10-15 minutes each)
Pick ONE based on student's mood and engagement level.

Extension A: Your Worst Parent Meeting

Tell me your worst parent meeting story.
What happened? How did you handle it? How did you WANT to handle it?

Pronunciation: Diplomatic Language

Practice saying these with controlled, professional tone:

"I understand your concern, but..."
(Firm but polite - stress on "understand" and "but")
"With all due respect..."
(Controlled frustration - careful emphasis)
"I hear what you're saying, however..."
(Professional disagreement - pause before "however")

Extension B: The One That Got Away

Tell me about a student you couldn't reach.
Every teacher has one. What happened? Do you still think about them? What would you do differently now?

Pronunciation: Regret & Reflection

Practice saying these with wistful, contemplative tone:

"If I could go back..."
(Wistful tone, falling intonation, slower pace)
"I wonder what would have happened if..."
(Contemplative pacing, quiet reflection)
"I still think about..."
(Quiet, personal tone - stress on "still")

Extension C: Debate - Should Burnt Out Teachers Quit?

Statement: "Teachers who feel burnt out should quit immediately, even mid-year, because staying hurts both teacher and students."
Activity:
1. Pick a side (agree or disagree)
2. Argue for 3 minutes
3. Switch sides, argue the opposite for 3 minutes
4. Discuss which argument felt stronger and why

Extension D: Role-Play - The Resignation Meeting

• Tone of voice (pleading, firm, exhausted)

Extension E: Quick Discussion Topics (5-7 minutes each)

Topic 1: Rate the Reasons
Sarah lists several reasons for quitting. Rank them from "I'd quit over this" to "I could handle this": difficult parents, lack of admin support, too many meetings, low pay, test score pressure, losing passion for teaching.
Topic 2: Letter to Sarah
If you could write Sarah a one-paragraph message right now, what would you say? Would you support her decision or try to change her mind?
Topic 3: Your Exit Plan
Do you have an exit plan? If you decided to quit teaching tomorrow, what would you do instead? Have you thought about it?
Topic 4: Then vs. Now
Compare yourself as a teacher in your first year versus now. What's changed? Are you better? Worse? Just different?
Topic 5: The Teacher You Remember
When you were a student, which teacher made the biggest impact on you? Why? Are you like them now?
