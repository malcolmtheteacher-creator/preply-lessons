Real Story #2: How The Algorithm Works

The Dopamine Trap

TikTok's algorithm learns what keeps YOU watching. If you pause on body transformation videos, it shows you more. If you watch mental health content about depression, it creates a feed that can actually reinforce anxiety and sadness.

Your brain releases dopamine (pleasure chemical) when you get likes, comments, new followers. The apps are literally designed to be addictive using the same mechanisms as slot machines.

6 C2 Expressions

1. "To exploit vulnerabilities"
Meaning:

To take advantage of weaknesses or insecurities for personal gain or profit

Context:

Used in: psychology, marketing, technology, critical analysis of systems

Why it matters:

This expression signals you understand that manipulation isn't always obviousâ€”it's often targeting specific psychological weaknesses

When NOT to use:

Don't use this for minor advantages or innocent situations. "Exploit" implies something unethical or manipulative.

Example 1 (Social Media Context):

"Social media companies exploit vulnerabilities in human psychologyâ€”our need for validation, our fear of missing out, and our addiction to novelty. They know exactly which buttons to push."

Example 2 (Academic Context):

"The marketing campaign exploited vulnerabilities in consumer decision-making by using time pressure and scarcity tactics."

Your turn to use it:

Can you think of other ways companies or apps exploit vulnerabilities? (Think: dating apps, video games, shopping websites)

2. "Algorithmic manipulation"
Meaning:

When systems or software are deliberately designed to control, influence, or alter human behavior

Context:

Used in: technology criticism, digital ethics, AI discussions, media studies

Why it's powerful:

This expression shows you understand that technology isn't neutralâ€”algorithms are designed with specific goals (usually maximizing engagement/profit)

Related terms:

Algorithmic bias, filter bubbles, recommendation systems, attention economy

Example 1 (TikTok):

"TikTok's algorithmic manipulation keeps users scrolling by learning exactly what content triggers dopamine release. The algorithm knows what you want to see before you do."

Example 2 (Academic Essay):

"Algorithmic manipulation raises ethical questions about consent. Users can't truly consent to manipulation they don't understand is happening."

Discussion point:

Is all algorithmic influence "manipulation," or only when it's hidden? What's the difference between helpful recommendations and manipulation?

3. "Dopamine-driven feedback loops"
Meaning:

Addictive reward cycles where behavior is reinforced by the release of pleasure chemicals in the brain

Context:

Used in: neuroscience, addiction studies, app design criticism, behavioral psychology

Why it's sophisticated:

This shows you understand the biological mechanism behind addiction, not just "people can't stop using apps"

The science:

Dopamine is a neurotransmitter that creates pleasure. Social media gives you small, unpredictable rewards (likes, comments) that keep your brain wanting moreâ€”same mechanism as gambling.

Example 1 (Instagram):

"Instagram likes create dopamine-driven feedback loopsâ€”you post, get validation, feel good, want to post again. Each notification is a tiny hit of pleasure that keeps you coming back."

Example 2 (Formal analysis):

"The dopamine-driven feedback loops engineered into social media platforms mirror the psychological mechanisms that make gambling addictive."

Critical thinking:

Are all feedback loops bad? Learning and improvement also use feedback loops. What makes social media's version problematic?

4. "Echo chambers"
Meaning:

Environments (often online) where you only encounter information and opinions that reinforce your existing beliefs

Context:

Used in: political discussions, media criticism, social media analysis, epistemology (study of knowledge)

Why it happens:

Algorithms show you content similar to what you've liked before. You follow people who think like you. Disagreement feels uncomfortable, so platforms minimize it.

Related concepts:

Filter bubbles, confirmation bias, epistemic closure, polarization

Example 1 (Social media):

"Social media creates echo chambers where you never encounter views that challenge your own. Your feed becomes a mirror that only reflects what you already believe."

Example 2 (Nuanced view):

"While echo chambers can increase polarization, they also provide safe spaces for marginalized communities. The issue is more complex than simply 'echo chambers bad.'"

Self-reflection:

When's the last time you encountered an opinion you strongly disagreed with? Do you actively seek opposing views, or avoid them?

5. "Cognitive bias"
Meaning:

Systematic errors in thinking that affect judgments and decisions

Context:

Used in: psychology, decision-making, critical thinking, argument analysis, research methods

Why it's essential at C2:

Understanding cognitive biases shows sophisticated thinking. You recognize that our brains aren't perfectly rationalâ€”we have predictable thinking errors.

Common types:

Confirmation bias (seeking info that confirms beliefs), availability bias (overvaluing recent/emotional info), anchoring bias (first info disproportionately influences us)

Example 1 (Analyzing arguments):

"The availability bias is a cognitive bias where we overestimate the importance of information that's recent or emotionally powerful. This is why one viral story can seem more important than statistical evidence."

Example 2 (Social media):

"Social media platforms exploit cognitive biases like our tendency to seek social validation and our fear of being left out."

Academic use:

Essential for analyzing arguments and evaluating evidence in essays and discussions. Shows you can think about thinking itself (metacognition).

6. "To curate a persona"
Meaning:

To carefully construct and maintain a particular image or identity (especially online)

Context:

Used in: social media analysis, identity studies, personal branding, performance theory

Etymology note:

"Curate" originally meant selecting items for a museum. Using it for personas emphasizes the deliberate, artistic construction of identity.

Why it's relevant:

Everyone does this to some degree online. The question is: where's the line between presentation and deception?

Example 1 (Influencers):

"Many influencers curate a persona that shows only success, beauty, and happinessâ€”hiding struggles, failures, and normal human imperfection."

Example 2 (Self-reflection):

"I realized I'd been curating a persona online that wasn't really me. I only posted when I looked good, was doing something interesting, or had something impressive to share."

Discussion question:

Is curating a persona dishonest, or is it just normal self-presentation? Everyone presents themselves differently in different contexts (with friends vs. family vs. teachers).

Vocabulary Set 2: Critical Analysis & Argumentation

ðŸŽ¯ These expressions = sophisticated thinking

These 6 expressions show you can analyze arguments, evaluate evidence, and present nuanced positions. This is university-level academic discourse.

6 C2 Expressions

7. "The evidence suggests that..."
Meaning:

Introducing claims based on research, data, or observable facts (not just personal opinion)

Context:

Used in: academic writing, formal presentations, research discussions, evidence-based arguments

Why it works:

Shows you're basing arguments on facts, not feelings. Signals intellectual rigor and critical thinking.

Important distinction:

Don't say "proves" unless evidence is conclusive. "Suggests" is more accurate and shows you understand research limitations.

Example 1 (Formal argument):

"The evidence suggests that excessive social media use correlates with increased anxiety in adolescents, though we must be careful not to confuse correlation with causation."

Example 2 (Responding to Emma's story):

"The evidence suggests that taking breaks from social media can improve mental health. Emma's experience aligns with research showing reduced anxiety after digital detoxes."

In your essays:

Use this when citing research, statistics, or studies. Much stronger than "I think" or "people say."

8. "One could argue that..."
Meaning:

Presenting a viewpoint without fully committing to it; acknowledging that this perspective exists and has merit

Context:

Used in: essays, debates, nuanced discussions where you want to show intellectual flexibility

Why it's sophisticated:

Shows you can consider multiple perspectives, even ones you don't personally agree with. This is critical thinking.

Common pattern:

Often followed by "However..." or "Conversely..." to present your actual position after acknowledging the opposition.

Example 1 (Balanced view):

"One could argue that social media provides valuable connection for isolated teenagers. However, the question is whether the benefits outweigh the documented mental health costs."

Example 2 (Devil's advocate):

"One could argue that teenagers are just complaining about social media because they lack self-control. But this ignores the deliberate design choices that make these platforms addictive."

In debates:

Use this to show you understand the opposing position before you demolish it with your better argument!

9. "While it's true that..., we must also consider..."
Meaning:

Acknowledging valid points before presenting counterarguments or additional factors

Context:

Used in: persuasive writing, formal debates, sophisticated discussions requiring nuance

Why it's powerful:

Shows you're not ignoring oppositionâ€”you've considered it and still have a stronger point. This is how experts argue.

Structure:

Concede reasonable point â†’ pivot with "but/however" â†’ present stronger counterpoint

Example 1 (Social media debate):

"While it's true that social media can harm mental health, we must also consider that it provides community and support for marginalized groups who might otherwise feel isolated."

Example 2 (Addiction argument):

"While it's true that people should have personal responsibility, we must also consider that these platforms are specifically engineered by teams of psychologists to be as addictive as possible."

Pro move:

This structure makes you sound reasonable and thoughtful, not dogmatic or close-minded.

10. "The correlation between X and Y"
Meaning:

Showing a relationship between two variables without claiming one causes the other

Context:

Used in: research discussion, data analysis, scientific thinking, argument evaluation

Critical distinction:

Correlation â‰  Causation. Just because two things happen together doesn't mean one causes the other.

Why this matters:

Shows you understand research methodology and can evaluate evidence critically. Essential for C2-level academic discourse.

Example 1 (Critical analysis):

"The correlation between social media use and depression doesn't necessarily prove causation. It's possible that depressed people simply use social media more, not that social media causes depression."

Example 2 (Evaluating research):

"Studies show a correlation between screen time and poor sleep, but we should be careful about assuming direct causation without considering other factors like caffeine consumption or stress."

Classic example to explain it:

Ice cream sales correlate with drowning deaths (both increase in summer). Does ice cream cause drowning? Noâ€”heat is the common factor.

11. "To conflate A with B"
Meaning:

To mistakenly treat two different things as if they're the same

Context:

Used in: argument analysis, identifying logical errors, critical thinking discussions

Use case:

Pointing out flaws in others' arguments (politely). Shows you can spot faulty reasoning.

Why it's powerful:

This is a sophisticated analytical move. Shows you understand distinctions others miss.

Example 1 (Screen time debate):

"People often conflate screen time with social media use, but watching Netflix isn't the same as scrolling Instagram. The activities have different psychological effects."

Example 2 (Critical analysis):

"We shouldn't conflate being connected online with having genuine connections. You can have 1,000 followers and still feel completely alone."

Common mistake to identify:

People conflate "feeling upset" with "being harmed." Discomfort isn't always damage. This distinction matters in debates.

12. "A more nuanced perspective would acknowledge..."
Meaning:

Introducing complexity and subtlety to oversimplified positions

Context:

Used in: academic discussions, debates, critical analysis, anywhere you want to show sophisticated thinking

Why it's C2-level:

This phrase literally signals "I'm not thinking in black and white, I understand complexity." It's a marker of intellectual maturity.

What "nuanced" means:

Subtle, detailed, recognizing small but important differences and exceptions

Example 1 (Social media impact):

"A more nuanced perspective would acknowledge that social media's impact varies dramatically based on how it's used. Passive scrolling differs from active engagement with communities."

Example 2 (Age matters):

"A more nuanced perspective would acknowledge that social media affects 13-year-olds differently than 18-year-olds. Developmental stage matters significantly."

When to use:

When someone presents a simple answer to a complex question. When you want to show the issue is more complicated than it appears.
