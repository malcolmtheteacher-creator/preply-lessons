Essential Reading Strategies

üéØ The Golden Rule: You don't have time to read like you're reading a novel. You need to read strategically for the test!

Click Each Strategy to Learn More

1Ô∏è‚É£

Read the Blurb First

The intro paragraph is gold!

Why it works:

The blurb (italic intro) tells you: the author, publication date, and topic. This gives you context BEFORE you dive in.

Example:

"This passage is adapted from a 2019 article about climate change policy by environmental scientist Dr. Sarah Chen."

Now you know: recent article, science topic, author's likely perspective!

2Ô∏è‚É£

Question-First Strategy

Look at questions BEFORE reading!

Why it works:

Quickly skim the questions (NOT the answer choices) to see what you need to focus on.

What to look for:
  • Line reference questions (lines 23-25)
  • Vocabulary questions ("as used in line...")
  • Main idea questions

Time cost: 30 seconds. Benefit: Know what to look for while reading!

3Ô∏è‚É£

Active Reading

Mark up your passage!

What to mark:
  • üìå Main ideas (usually first/last sentences of paragraphs)
  • üîÑ Transition words (however, although, furthermore)
  • ‚ùó Strong opinions or emotional language
  • üìä Data, statistics, or examples
  • ‚ùì Lines referenced in questions

Pro tip: You can write in your test booklet. Use it!

4Ô∏è‚É£

Process of Elimination (POE)

Your most powerful tool!

Cross off answers that are:
  • ‚ùå Too extreme ("always," "never," "only")
  • ‚ùå Not mentioned in the passage at all
  • ‚ùå Opposite of what the passage says
  • ‚ùå Half right, half wrong (the whole answer must be correct!)

Reality: Often you won't LOVE an answer. That's okay! Pick the least wrong option.

5Ô∏è‚É£

Answer in Your Own Words First

Don't let choices confuse you!

The technique:

Before looking at A, B, C, D - answer the question in YOUR head first. Then find which choice matches your thinking.

Example:

Question: "What is the author's main purpose?"

Your thought: "To warn people about climate change risks"

Then look for: The answer that matches your prediction!

Why it works: Prevents the test from manipulating you with tricky wording!

6Ô∏è‚É£

Go Back to the Passage

Don't rely on memory!

The rule:

ALWAYS return to the passage to verify your answer. Your memory is not reliable under time pressure!

How to do it:
  • Line reference? Read 2-3 lines before AND after
  • No line reference? Think about which paragraph discusses this topic
  • Between two answers? Find evidence in the text for each, pick the stronger one

Time cost: 10 seconds to look back. Benefit: Getting it RIGHT!

üí≠ Main Idea / Purpose Questions

How to recognize them:
  • "The main idea of the passage is..."
  • "The author's primary purpose is to..."
  • "The passage is primarily concerned with..."
  • "Which statement best summarizes..."

Strategy:

  1. Read first & last paragraphs carefully - These usually contain the main idea
  2. Look for thesis statements - Often in first paragraph
  3. Use POE to eliminate:
    • ‚ùå Too narrow (only talks about one detail)
    • ‚ùå Too broad (could apply to any passage)
    • ‚ùå Not mentioned at all
    • ‚úÖ Covers the whole passage without being vague
üí° Pro Tip: Ask yourself: "If I had to explain this passage to a friend in ONE sentence, what would I say?" That's probably the main idea!

üîç Detail / Evidence Questions

How to recognize them:
  • "According to the passage..."
  • "The author states that..."
  • "Which choice provides the best evidence..."
  • "In lines 23-27, the author mentions..."

Strategy:

  1. Always has line references (given or you need to find)
  2. Go directly to those lines - don't rely on memory!
  3. Read context: 2-3 lines BEFORE and AFTER the reference
  4. Match exact meaning: The answer will be a paraphrase of what's in the passage
  5. Watch for trap answers:
    • ‚ùå True in real life, but not in THIS passage
    • ‚ùå Uses same words but different meaning
    • ‚ùå Mentioned in passage but doesn't answer THIS question
üí° Pro Tip: These are the "easiest" questions because the answer is literally IN the passage. Never guess - always find the evidence!

üí° Inference / Implication Questions

How to recognize them:
  • "It can reasonably be inferred..."
  • "The passage suggests that..."
  • "The author implies that..."
  • "Based on the passage, the reader can conclude..."

Strategy:

  1. Key concept: "Inference" doesn't mean wild guessing! It means "reading between the lines"
  2. The answer must be supported by the passage even if not directly stated
  3. Use POE aggressively:
    • ‚ùå Too big a leap (requires too much assuming)
    • ‚ùå Contradicts the passage
    • ‚ùå Could be true, but no support in passage
    • ‚úÖ Logically follows from what's written
  4. Look for: Tone, cause-effect, comparisons, author's attitude
üí° Pro Tip: Think of it like a detective: "What can I conclude based ONLY on the evidence given?" Don't bring in outside knowledge!

üìö Vocabulary in Context Questions

How to recognize them:
  • "As used in line 34, 'critical' most nearly means..."
  • "In the context of the passage, 'novel' (line 12) refers to..."
  • "The word 'bear' in line 56 is closest in meaning to..."

Strategy:

  1. NEVER just pick the most common meaning! SAT tests if you understand context
  2. Cover the word with your hand - What word would YOU use there?
  3. Plug each answer back into the sentence - Which fits best?
  4. Read surrounding sentences for more context
  5. Example:

    "The novel approach surprised researchers."
    "Novel" here means: new/innovative (not a book!)

üí° Pro Tip: SAT loves words with multiple meanings. The "obvious" definition is usually WRONG. Always check context!

This passage is adapted from a 2020 article on urban planning and public health by Dr. Maria Rodriguez.

(1) In cities around the world, urban planners are increasingly recognizing the critical importance of green spaces‚Äîparks, gardens, and tree-lined streets‚Äîin promoting both environmental sustainability and public well-being. (5) While the aesthetic appeal of these spaces is immediately apparent, their benefits extend far beyond simple beautification.

(10) Research has demonstrated that access to urban green spaces significantly improves mental health outcomes. A comprehensive study conducted in the United Kingdom found that residents living within walking distance of parks reported 15% lower stress levels (15) compared to those without such access. The mechanisms behind this effect are multifaceted: exposure to nature reduces cortisol levels, provides opportunities for physical exercise, and facilitates social interaction within communities.

(20) Moreover, green spaces play a crucial role in mitigating urban environmental challenges. Trees and vegetation absorb carbon dioxide and release oxygen, helping to combat air pollution that plagues many metropolitan areas. During extreme (25) heat events, which are becoming more frequent due to climate change, parks can be several degrees cooler than surrounding concrete surfaces, providing vital refuge for vulnerable populations.

(30) Critics might argue that dedicating valuable urban land to parks represents an inefficient use of space that could otherwise accommodate housing or commercial development. However, this perspective fails to account for the long-term (35) economic benefits of green spaces. Property values near parks typically appreciate more rapidly than comparable properties elsewhere, and businesses often report increased customer traffic in areas with attractive public spaces. Additionally, the healthcare savings (40) resulting from improved public health may offset the initial investment in park development.

(45) The challenge facing contemporary cities is not whether to invest in green spaces, but how to ensure equitable access to these resources. Lower-income neighborhoods often have significantly less green space per capita than affluent areas, a disparity that compounds existing (50) health inequities. Progressive urban planning must prioritize the creation of accessible green spaces in underserved communities, recognizing that environmental justice is inseparable from social justice.

Mastering Time Management

‚è∞ The Reality: Time management is just as important as reading comprehension. You can know all the strategies, but if you run out of time, your score suffers!

The Time Allocation Strategy

üìñ Reading Phase

6-8

minutes per passage

  • Skim questions: 30 sec
  • Read passage: 5-6 min
  • Mark key points: ongoing
  • Quick summary: 30 sec

‚ùì Question Phase

5-7

minutes per passage

  • 10-11 questions total
  • ~30-45 sec per question
  • Easy ones: 20 sec
  • Hard ones: 1 min max

‚ö° Buffer Time

2-3

minutes at the end

  • Review marked questions
  • Fill in any blanks
  • Double-check bubbling
  • Stay calm!

Time Management Rules

‚úÖ DO:
  • Wear a watch - Your testing room may not have a clock!
  • Check time after each passage - Stay on track throughout
  • Mark and move on - Stuck for >1 minute? Circle it and come back
  • Answer everything - No penalty for wrong answers = always guess!
  • Start with your strongest passage type - Build confidence first
‚ùå DON'T:
  • Spend >2 minutes on any single question
  • Read passages out of order without a strategy
  • Leave questions blank (guess if you must!)
  • Panic if you're behind - stay focused on one question at a time
  • Rush through easy questions to "save time" - these are free points!

üí° Pro Strategy: Passage Selection

You DON'T have to do passages in order! Quick scan all 5 passages and identify:

  • Your "easy win" - Start here for confidence
  • Your "hardest" - Do this 2nd or 3rd while you're fresh, NOT last!
  • Paired passages - Usually take longest, plan accordingly

Remember to bubble answers in the RIGHT place if you skip around!
