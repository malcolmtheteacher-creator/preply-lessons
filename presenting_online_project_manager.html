Opening & Navigating Issues

Click each expression to see how to use it

1. "Before we dive in, let me quickly set the context..."
Professional
When to use: Opening your presentation - this signals you're about to start and focuses attention.

Why it works: "Before we dive in" acknowledges everyone's time and signals urgency. "Set the context" prepares people to understand what follows. Perfect for when people are still settling in or joining late.
In Sarah's sprint review:
"Before we dive in, let me quickly set the context. We're two weeks into Sprint 12, and I know some of you are joining from back-to-back meetings, so I'll keep this focused on the three blockers that need your input today."
NOW YOU TRY: You're presenting a project status update. Half the team just joined. Use this expression to open your presentation and focus everyone's attention on what matters today.
2. "For those just joining, here's where we are..."
Professional
When to use: When people join late (which happens constantly in virtual meetings).

Why it works: Acknowledges latecomers without making them feel guilty. Gets everyone on the same page. Shows you're organized and inclusive. Prevents the awkward "should I catch them up?" moment.
James handling a late CEO:
"For those just joining, here's where we are - we've covered the budget variance, and we're now discussing the timeline implications. The key decision we need today is whether to push the deadline or add resources."
NOW YOU TRY: You're 10 minutes into a presentation when the CTO joins. Use this expression to catch them up in 20 seconds without losing momentum with others.
3. "Bear with me for a moment while I..."
Professional / Friendly
When to use: Technical difficulties, switching screens, pulling up documents, fixing audio - any awkward pause.

Why it works: Acknowledges the delay, asks for patience politely, explains what you're doing. Prevents awkward silence. Shows you're in control even when things go wrong.
Sarah's screen share fails:
"Bear with me for a moment while I share my screen again - seems like Zoom is having other ideas. While we wait, I can tell you that the key metric we'll be looking at is the velocity trend from the last three sprints."
NOW YOU TRY: Your slides won't load. Use this expression to maintain professionalism while you fix the issue, and fill the time by previewing what people will see.

Engaging & Closing

4. "I'd love to hear your thoughts on this..."
Professional / Collaborative
When to use: Inviting input, asking for opinions, getting quiet stakeholders to participate.

Why it works: "I'd love to" shows genuine interest (not obligation). "Your thoughts" is more open than "your opinion" or "questions". Creates psychological safety for people to speak up. Particularly powerful when you name specific people.
James getting C-level input:
"So those are the three options for handling the delay. I'd love to hear your thoughts on this, particularly from Sarah and Tom, since this directly impacts your teams. What approach makes the most sense from your perspective?"
NOW YOU TRY: You've just presented a complex technical decision. The call is silent - everyone on mute with cameras off. Use this expression to specifically invite the Lead Developer and Product Owner to share their perspective.
5. "Just to wrap up, the key takeaways are..."
Professional
When to use: Closing your presentation, summarizing next steps, ensuring clarity before ending.

Why it works: "Just to wrap up" signals you're finishing (re-engages people who zoned out). "Key takeaways" focuses on what matters most. Prevents the awkward "So... I guess we're done?" ending. Ensures everyone leaves with clear understanding.
Sarah closing her sprint review:
"Just to wrap up, the key takeaways are: Sprint 12 is on track, we need approval for the API vendor by Friday, and the three blockers I showed need owner assignments. I'll send those action items in Slack within the hour. Any final questions?"
NOW YOU TRY: You've spent 40 minutes discussing a complicated project pivot. Time is running out and you need to ensure everyone knows the 3 decisions made and who owns what next. Use this expression to close strong.
