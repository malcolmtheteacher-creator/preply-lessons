Vocabulary Set 1: Environmental & Political Discourse

üìö Climate & Justice Vocabulary

These 6 expressions allow you to discuss climate change with the sophistication it deserves‚Äîaddressing systemic issues, responsibility, and the political/economic dimensions of environmental crisis.

6 C2 Expressions

1. "Systemic change vs. individual action"
Meaning:

Systemic change: Changing the structure, rules, and incentives of systems (government policy, corporate regulation, economic models)
Individual action: Personal choices and behaviors (recycling, veganism, using less plastic)

Context:

Used in: climate activism, social justice, political reform discussions

The debate:

Can individual actions solve systemic problems? Or do we need to change the system itself? Many argue focusing on individual action lets corporations and governments off the hook.

Why it matters:

This distinction is crucial for understanding power and responsibility. Are you the problem, or is the system the problem?

Example 1 (Climate debate):

"Recycling is individual action, but it won't solve climate change. We need systemic change‚Äîregulations forcing corporations to reduce emissions, carbon taxes, banning fossil fuel subsidies."

Example 2 (Nuanced view):

"The debate between systemic change and individual action creates a false dichotomy. We need both: policy reform to regulate corporations AND cultural shifts that make sustainable living the norm."

Your turn:

Where do YOU stand? Do your individual choices matter, or is that just distraction from demanding systemic change?

2. "Greenwashing"
Meaning:

When companies or organizations falsely present themselves as environmentally responsible while continuing harmful practices

Context:

Used in: corporate criticism, marketing analysis, environmental activism

How it works:

Companies spend more on advertising their "green" image than on actual environmental improvements. They highlight small positive actions while hiding massive negative impacts.

Examples:

‚Ä¢ Oil companies with ads about renewable energy (while drilling more oil)
‚Ä¢ "Eco-friendly" packaging on products shipped halfway around the world
‚Ä¢ Corporate "carbon offset" programs that don't actually reduce emissions

Example 1 (Corporate criticism):

"BP's 'Beyond Petroleum' campaign was pure greenwashing‚Äîthey rebranded as an eco-friendly company while still investing 96% of their capital in fossil fuels."

Example 2 (Consumer awareness):

"Most 'sustainable' fashion brands are guilty of greenwashing. They release one 'conscious collection' made from recycled materials while their main production still exploits workers and damages the environment."

Critical thinking:

How can you tell the difference between genuine environmental commitment and greenwashing? What evidence would you look for?

3. "Intergenerational injustice"
Meaning:

Unfairness between different age groups, where one generation benefits at the expense of future generations

Context:

Used in: climate ethics, social justice, economic policy, environmental philosophy

The climate connection:

Older generations enjoyed cheap fossil fuels and economic growth. Younger generations inherit the consequences (climate disaster, economic costs of adaptation, environmental collapse).

Why it's a justice issue:

Those who caused the problem largely won't experience the worst effects. Those who will suffer the most (young people, future generations) had no say in the decisions that created the crisis.

Example 1 (Climate justice):

"Climate change is a clear case of intergenerational injustice. Baby boomers benefited from cheap fossil fuels and economic growth, while Gen Z will pay the price through climate disasters, water scarcity, and mass migration."

Example 2 (Greta's argument):

"Greta Thunberg's 'How dare you' speech was fundamentally about intergenerational injustice‚Äîcalling out adults for stealing young people's futures while knowing the consequences of their inaction."

Discussion point:

Does intergenerational injustice make you angry? Should older generations be held accountable? How?

4. "Carbon footprint"
Meaning:

A measure of the total greenhouse gas emissions caused by an individual, organization, event, or product

Context:

Used in: environmental discussions, sustainability, climate action

Controversial history:

The term "carbon footprint" was popularized by BP (British Petroleum) in a 2000s marketing campaign to shift focus from corporate emissions to individual responsibility.

The problem:

Focusing on individual carbon footprints can distract from the fact that corporations and governments are responsible for the vast majority of emissions.

Example 1 (Individual focus):

"Calculating your carbon footprint can help you make more sustainable choices‚Äîlike flying less, eating less meat, or choosing renewable energy sources."

Example 2 (Critical perspective):

"The obsession with individual carbon footprints is problematic when 100 companies are responsible for 71% of emissions. This deflects attention from corporate accountability to individual guilt."

Nuanced view:

Can BOTH be true? Individual carbon footprints matter AND corporate emissions need regulation? Or is one clearly more important?

5. "Regulatory capture"
Meaning:

When industries or corporations gain excessive influence over the government agencies meant to regulate them

Context:

Used in: political science, corruption analysis, industry critique

How it works:

Former industry executives get appointed to regulatory agencies. Regulators are influenced by lobbying, campaign donations, or promises of future jobs. The result: regulations that benefit industries instead of protecting the public.

Climate example:

Fossil fuel companies have enormous influence over environmental policy, making meaningful climate regulation nearly impossible.

Example 1 (Political analysis):

"Regulatory capture explains why environmental protections are so weak‚Äîfossil fuel companies have captured the agencies meant to regulate them through lobbying and campaign finance."

Example 2 (Specific case):

"When the former CEO of ExxonMobil was appointed U.S. Secretary of State, many saw it as evidence of regulatory capture‚Äîthe fossil fuel industry literally controlling government policy."

Critical question:

If regulatory capture is real, how can we expect governments to regulate industries effectively? What's the solution?

6. "Existential threat"
Meaning:

A danger to existence itself; something that threatens the survival or fundamental nature of humanity or civilization

Context:

Used in: crisis discussions, security analysis, philosophical debates

Climate as existential threat:

Climate change threatens human civilization as we know it‚Äînot necessarily extinction, but collapse of social structures, economies, and ways of life.

Why the term matters:

Calling something an "existential threat" communicates maximum urgency. It says: this is THE biggest issue, nothing else matters if we don't solve this.

Example 1 (Climate urgency):

"Climate change is an existential threat to human civilization. Without immediate action, we're looking at societal collapse, mass extinction events, and the end of life as we know it."

Example 2 (Debate perspective):

"Some argue that calling climate change an existential threat creates panic and paralysis. Others say it's the only language strong enough to convey the actual danger we face."

Reflection:

Do YOU think climate change is an existential threat? Does that term feel accurate, or exaggerated? Why?

Vocabulary Set 2: Argumentation & Persuasion

üéØ Debate & Evidence Language

These 6 expressions help you build persuasive arguments, address counterarguments, and discuss who should be responsible for solving complex problems.

6 C2 Expressions

7. "The burden of proof lies with..."
Meaning:

The responsibility to provide evidence belongs to a specific person, group, or side of an argument

Context:

Used in: debates, legal discussions, scientific method, argumentation theory

Why it matters:

Determining who must prove their case is crucial. The side making a claim usually has the burden of proof.

Climate application:

Should climate scientists prove that climate change is happening? Or should deniers prove it's NOT happening? Who has the burden?

Example 1 (Climate debate):

"The burden of proof lies with those claiming climate change isn't caused by humans. The scientific consensus is overwhelming‚Äîdeniers must provide extraordinary evidence for their extraordinary claim."

Example 2 (Corporate responsibility):

"The burden of proof lies with fossil fuel companies to demonstrate that their operations won't cause irreversible environmental harm. It shouldn't be on society to prove they WILL cause harm."

In debates:

Use this to assign responsibility for providing evidence. It's a powerful rhetorical move.

8. "A false dichotomy"
Meaning:

Presenting only two options when more alternatives exist; a logical fallacy that oversimplifies complex issues

Context:

Used in: argument analysis, critical thinking, logic, debate

Also called:

"False binary," "either-or fallacy," "black-and-white thinking"

Why recognize it:

Politicians, marketers, and activists often use false dichotomies to simplify issues and force people to pick sides. Spotting this fallacy shows sophisticated thinking.

Example 1 (Climate discussion):

"The debate between individual action and systemic change is a false dichotomy. We don't have to choose one or the other‚Äîwe need both personal responsibility AND policy reform."

Example 2 (Identifying the fallacy):

"Saying 'either we ban all fossil fuels immediately or we accept climate collapse' is a false dichotomy. There are gradual transition strategies, renewable energy development, and various policy approaches between these extremes."

Critical thinking:

When you hear "either X or Y," ask yourself: Are there really only two options? What alternatives are being ignored?

9. "To shift the onus onto..."
Meaning:

To transfer responsibility or burden from one party to another

Context:

Used in: responsibility discussions, power analysis, accountability debates

What's "onus"?:

Onus = burden or responsibility. It's a formal term that emphasizes duty and obligation.

Climate relevance:

Who should bear the burden of fixing climate change? This expression helps analyze power dynamics and responsibility shifting.

Example 1 (Corporate deflection):

"BP's carbon footprint campaign was designed to shift the onus onto individual consumers, deflecting attention from corporate emissions and systemic problems."

Example 2 (Generational dynamics):

"Older generations who caused climate change are now trying to shift the onus onto young people to solve it‚Äîwithout giving them political power or economic resources to do so."

Power analysis:

This expression reveals how power works‚Äîthose with power often shift responsibility to those without it.

10. "The moral imperative"
Meaning:

An ethical obligation so strong that you MUST act; something morally required, not just preferred

Context:

Used in: ethics, activism, moral philosophy, urgent social issues

Why "imperative"?:

Imperative = absolutely necessary, urgent, compelling. Stronger than "should"‚Äîit's a MUST.

Rhetorical power:

Calling something a "moral imperative" raises stakes. It says this isn't about preferences or costs‚Äîit's about fundamental right and wrong.

Example 1 (Climate ethics):

"Addressing climate change is a moral imperative. We have no right to destroy the planet our children will inherit. Economic concerns, while valid, cannot override this ethical obligation."

Example 2 (Urgency argument):

"When we face a moral imperative, cost-benefit analysis becomes irrelevant. You don't ask 'Can we afford to save lives?' You ask 'How can we NOT act?'"

Debate use:

This frames an issue as non-negotiable. It's powerful rhetoric, but also open to criticism if overused.

11. "Incrementalism vs. radical change"
Meaning:

Incrementalism: Gradual, step-by-step reforms; making small changes over time
Radical change: Dramatic, rapid transformation; fundamentally restructuring systems

Context:

Used in: political strategy, social movements, reform debates

The debate:

Incrementalists say: Gradual change is realistic and sustainable. Radicals say: We don't have time for baby steps; we need revolution.

Climate application:

Do we slowly transition away from fossil fuels? Or do we need immediate, dramatic action like the Green New Deal?

Example 1 (Strategy debate):

"The choice between incrementalism and radical change is crucial for climate policy. Some argue for gradual carbon taxes; others demand immediate fossil fuel bans and massive renewable investment."

Example 2 (Time pressure):

"Incrementalism might work for normal problems, but climate change requires radical change. When the house is on fire, you don't gradually reduce the flames‚Äîyou act immediately and dramatically."

Discussion point:

Which approach do YOU think is better for climate change? Why?

12. "To hold accountable"
Meaning:

To make someone responsible for their actions and ensure they face consequences

Context:

Used in: justice discussions, governance, corporate responsibility, ethics

Why it matters:

Accountability means consequences. Without it, promises and policies are meaningless.

Climate challenge:

How do you hold corporations, governments, or entire generations accountable for environmental destruction?

Example 1 (Corporate responsibility):

"We must hold corporations accountable for their emissions. This means carbon taxes, legal liability for environmental damage, and criminal prosecution for executives who knowingly caused harm."

Example 2 (Political challenge):

"The difficulty is holding accountable people who won't live to see the consequences of their actions. How do you hold a generation accountable for intergenerational injustice?"

Practical question:

What would real accountability look like for climate destruction? Fines? Prison? Asset seizure? Something else?
